=== SEMANTIC REVIEW: soft-bound-traditions-as-sources-for-context-engineering-strategies.md ===

Claims identified: 13

WARN:
- [Completeness] The note claims "twelve such traditions" in the opening paragraph, but the table lists exactly twelve rows. However, the transfer assessment section accounts for only 5 + 3 + 4 = 12 items — but these are *strategies*, not traditions. Several traditions are merged into single transfer items (e.g., "Front-load important information (from journalism, technical writing)" collapses two traditions into one strategy, while "Atomic composable units (from Zettelkasten, modular design)" collapses two more). Meanwhile "Modular design (Parnas)" appears twice — once as "Decompose into independent contexts" and once folded into "Atomic composable units." The result: some traditions appear in multiple transfer items, some share a single item, and journalism appears as a source ("from journalism, technical writing") despite not being listed as one of the twelve traditions in the table. The mapping from traditions to transfer items is not one-to-one and the note does not acknowledge this.

- [Completeness] The five structural elements are presented as recurring "across these traditions," but element 4 — "Progressive formalization — start loose, tighten as patterns emerge" — is a poor fit for several traditions in the table. Attention economics (Simon) is about filtering and satisficing, not progressive tightening. Working memory (Miller, Cowan) operates via chunking and rehearsal, not formalization. Zettelkasten linking is judgment-based but not progressive-formalization in any standard sense. Organizational learning (Argyris & Schon) revises governing assumptions — which is closer to restructuring than progressive formalization. The claim that all five elements "recur across these traditions" overstates the coverage of element 4.

- [Grounding] The distillation note says "the context budget is a hard constraint, not a soft guideline" (Prior work section). The traditions note's entire premise is that agent context is *soft-bounded* — the opening sentence links to the soft-degradation note and the "What blocks transfer" section depends on the soft-bound framing. The distillation note's characterization of the context budget as a "hard constraint" uses "hard" to mean "binding/non-negotiable" rather than "hard token limit," but this vocabulary mismatch with the traditions note's careful hard/soft distinction could mislead readers who follow the link. The note's claim that distillation has "roots in pedagogy, technical writing, knowledge management" (Relevant Notes section) is accurate per the distillation note's Prior work section.

- [Completeness] The "What blocks transfer" section claims "Three structural differences" limit transfer. A fourth candidate blocker is visible in the note's own material but not listed: **temporality mismatch**. Many traditions (organizational learning, knowledge creation, pedagogy, Zettelkasten) assume iterative, long-duration interaction between processor and knowledge — learning unfolds over time. Single-context agents have no temporal dimension. The note partially captures this under "Feedback absence" (blocker 2), but feedback and temporal iteration are distinct. Multi-turn agents partially satisfy the feedback requirement but still lack the months-to-years timescale that organizational learning and Zettelkasten assume. The note's own open question — "For aspirational traditions... is transfer truly blocked or just harder? Multi-turn agents with external evaluation might satisfy the feedback requirement" — hints at this gap without naming it.

INFO:
- [Completeness] The "Already transferred and working" tier claims these strategies are "already standard practice." For most this is clearly true (front-loading, chunking, decomposition). However, "Atomic composable units" — attributed to Zettelkasten and modular design — is standard in some KB-oriented agent systems but not standard across the field. Many production agent systems use monolithic system prompts or large document retrieval chunks, not atomic composable notes. The claim may be better described as "transferred in KB-oriented agent systems" rather than "standard practice."

- [Completeness] The boundary between "Plausible but untested" and "Aspirational — transfer conditions unclear" is not crisply defined. The note distinguishes them as "structural analogs exist" vs. "mechanisms may not survive transfer." But "Typed relationships" (plausible tier) faces the same feedback-absence problem as the aspirational strategies — you cannot observe whether typed links improve agent navigation without the same external evaluation that scaffolding and mastery learning need. The tier assignment may reflect author confidence more than a structural criterion.

- [Grounding] The deploy-time learning note's Related Work section says "Deploy-time learning is double-loop learning for agent systems — constraining revises the rules, not just the outputs" and maps SECI phases to constraining and distillation. The traditions note places both double-loop learning and SECI in the "Aspirational" tier, arguing transfer conditions are unclear. These two notes are in tension: deploy-time learning claims the transfer has already happened (at least partially), while the traditions note says transfer conditions are unclear. Both positions are defensible — the traditions note may be evaluating direct strategy transfer while deploy-time learning claims structural/analogical transfer — but the disagreement is not surfaced.

- [Internal consistency] The note says "Shared structure doesn't guarantee transfer" (end of "The traditions" section) but then organizes the transfer assessment partly on the basis of shared structure — the "plausible" tier is defined by having "structural analogs." This is not a contradiction (structural analogy is necessary but not sufficient), but the note could be clearer about what additional criteria distinguish the tiers.

PASS:
- [Internal consistency] The three blockers (optimization target mismatch, feedback absence, different failure modes) are internally consistent — each addresses a different dimension and they do not contradict each other. The aspirational strategies each map cleanly to at least one blocker: scaffolding/ZPD to feedback absence, double-loop to feedback absence, SECI to a domain mismatch (tacit knowledge), mastery learning to feedback absence. No aspirational item lacks a blocking explanation.

- [Grounding] The soft-degradation note confirms the note's foundational claim. The soft-degradation note says "the binding constraint is soft degradation, not hard token limits" and explicitly links back to this traditions note as "extends: if the binding constraint is a soft bound, these traditions have relevant coping strategies." The bidirectional linking is clean and the attribution is accurate.

- [Grounding] The constraining note's Prior work section lists "Gradual typing (Siek & Taha, 2006)" and "Formal specification (Z, VDM, TLA+)" as prior work, and says "The closest direct analogue" for gradual typing. The traditions note's claim that progressive formalization comes "from gradual typing, formal specification" and that the constraining methodology is "explicitly modeled on this" is accurately grounded.

- [Grounding] The context-efficiency note's Prior work section lists "Attention economics (Simon, 1971)," "Working memory (Miller, 1956; Cowan, 2001)," and "Information overload (Toffler, 1970)." The traditions note's attribution of context efficiency to "roots in Simon, working memory" matches. (Toffler is absent from the traditions note's table, but Information overload is arguably subsumed by Attention economics in this framing.)

- [Internal consistency] The note's definition of the shared problem — "a bounded processor that cannot consume all available knowledge must select, compress, and structure what it loads" — is used consistently throughout. The table's "Bounded processor" and "The bound" columns map each tradition to this definition, and the transfer assessment evaluates each tradition against agent context bounds. No definition drift detected.

Overall: 4 warnings, 4 info

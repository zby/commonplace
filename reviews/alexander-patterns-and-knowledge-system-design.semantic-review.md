- [Completeness / boundary cases] The note claims Alexander's generative process maps to the codification trajectory: "start with unstructured text, let patterns emerge from practice, formalise when consistent success becomes observable." But Alexander's generative process is about sequential placement decisions responding to local conditions and strengthening existing structure — it is a construction-time sequencing method, not an emergence-and-formalize method. The codification trajectory (observe pattern, then formalize) is closer to grounded theory or empirical generalization than to Alexander's generative sequences, which prescribe an order of operations for building, not a method for discovering what to build. The mapping conflates "responding to local conditions" with "letting patterns emerge," which are distinct processes: one is adaptive execution, the other is inductive discovery.

- [Completeness / boundary cases] The note organizes the Alexander connection into three levels — "Concrete: patterns as document types," "Structural: generative processes vs master plans," and "Vague: centers strengthening centers" — presented as a hierarchy of decreasing concreteness. Boundary case: Alexander's concept of "pattern forces" (the competing constraints a pattern resolves) has no mapping in the note. Forces are arguably the most distinctive feature of Alexander's pattern language — they explain *why* the solution takes its form. In a KB context, forces might map to the competing constraints a note resolves (context budget vs completeness, specificity vs reusability). This is a significant omission because the note claims to cover "multiple levels" of connection but misses the level most unique to Alexander's contribution.

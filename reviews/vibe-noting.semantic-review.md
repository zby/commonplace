WARN:
- [Completeness] The two-axes framework (inspectability x verifiability) is presented as the complete decomposition of what makes LLM-assisted work productive: "This suggests two independent axes for LLM-assisted work." But the note's own linked note on reach (ephemerality-is-safe-where-embedded-operational-knowledge-has-low-reach.md) identifies reach as a third factor that explains *when* inspectability becomes worth paying for. Reach is not reducible to either axis — low-reach code is inspectable and verifiable yet tolerates discard, while high-reach knowledge work demands persistence even when verifiability remains low. The two-axes framing appears incomplete against the note's own network.
- [Grounding — scope mismatch] The note says "code has hard oracles (tests) which is why it *automates*." The boundary-of-automation source note makes a more qualified claim: verification cost is "the primary structural determinant," with five caveats including error-cost tolerance, oracle gaming, and non-verification barriers (regulatory, trust, liability). The vibe-noting note drops all caveats and presents the relationship as definitional ("which is why"), upgrading a qualified structural claim to an unqualified causal one.
- [Completeness — boundary case: inspectable but unstructured artifacts] The note claims "Most knowledge work doesn't produce artifacts like that" (stored, structured, inspectable). But many knowledge workers do produce durable artifacts — academic papers, design documents, policy memos, legal briefs. These are stored and inspectable. What they typically lack is *linkage and machine-navigable structure*, not inspectability per se. The contrast is drawn too sharply: the real gap the KB fills is not "artifacts vs. no artifacts" but "linked, structured, navigable artifacts vs. isolated documents." The note partly addresses this with "documents nobody can navigate" but then re-frames the KB contribution as adding "structured, linked, readable artifacts" as if the problem were absence rather than disconnection.

INFO:
- [Grounding — vocabulary mismatch] The note uses "epiplexity" parenthetically when discussing reverse-compression: "the article carries no more epiplexity than the original sentence." The linked information-value-is-observer-relative note defines epiplexity as "epistemic complexity extractable by a bounded observer," drawing on a formal source (the epiplexity paper). The vibe-noting note uses the term casually as a synonym for "information content" without specifying the observer. This is a minor mismatch — the inference is reasonable (the intended observer is presumably the KB reader), but the term's formal dependency on specifying the observer is elided.
- [Completeness — boundary case: inspectable but not structured] Consider a plain-text personal journal or a flat folder of markdown files with no links. These are stored, inspectable artifacts — an agent can read them. But they lack the structure and linkage the note argues is the key enabler. The note's argument conflates "inspectable" with "structured and linked." The two-axes framework names the axis "inspectability" but the KB's actual contribution is closer to "structured navigability." This is related to the WARN above about the two-axes framework's completeness.
- [Internal consistency — tension between flywheel claim and weak-claim framing] The note invokes Kirsch to limit its own scope to the "weak sense" — augmentation gets cheaper, not that durable knowledge work disappears. Yet the flywheel claim ("the accumulated structure makes each subsequent session more productive") is an unbounded positive trajectory claim. The note adds a negative-compounding caveat, but the flywheel framing still implies monotonic improvement under good practices, which is stronger than the weak-sense framing would warrant. Whether the flywheel reliably compounds positively (rather than plateauing, or requiring increasing maintenance) is left as an assumption.
- [Internal consistency — "independent" axes may not be independent] The note calls inspectability and verifiability "two independent axes." But the note itself shows a dependency: "The weak oracles that make KB automation hard also mean there's no cheap way to detect this degradation" — i.e., low verifiability undermines the ability to maintain inspectability quality over time. If verifiability affects the long-run quality of inspectable artifacts, the axes are coupled, not independent. The note acknowledges this tension implicitly but the "independent" label overstates the separation.

PASS:
- [Grounding — Kirsch attribution] The note's characterization of Kirsch's argument is accurate. The ingest confirms Kirsch distinguishes vibe coding (the weak claim: cheap generation of disposable prototypes) from ephemeral software (the strong claim: important systems become safely forgettable). The note correctly attributes the weak/strong distinction and correctly positions vibe-noting as operating in the weak sense.
- [Grounding — boundary-of-automation attribution] The note explicitly flags that "this framing is the note's own contribution — the boundary-of-automation note covers only the verification axis." This is accurate — the boundary-of-automation note discusses inspectability nowhere and does not propose a two-axes framework. The attribution is honest and correctly scoped.
- [Grounding — reverse-compression attribution] The note's use of reverse-compression aligns with the linked reverse-compression note's definition: "expanding a compact signal into verbose prose without adding extractable structure." The characterization that links must be "load-bearing, not decorative" matches the source note's test: "remove the links — does the argument collapse?"
- [Internal consistency — negative compounding] The note acknowledges negative compounding ("poorly written or incorrectly linked notes can mislead a stateless agent, making orientation worse than starting fresh") in the same section where it makes the flywheel claim. This is a genuine tension that the note surfaces rather than hides, and the verification section reinforces it by noting weak oracles cannot cheaply detect degradation.
- [Internal consistency — process section] The "Origin: process illustration" section is consistent with the body. It describes a semantic review that caught three issues (negative compounding, overstated grounding, reversed link mapping), and all three fixes are visible in the current note body. The section does not contradict any claims made earlier.

=== SEMANTIC REVIEW: session-history-should-not-be-the-default-next-context.md ===

Claims identified: 18

WARN:
- [Completeness] The three-trace-type taxonomy ("conversation transcripts," "tool/action traces," "reasoning traces") is presented as decomposing what "history" conflates ("at least three trace types"). Boundary case: **decision/judgment artifacts** — when an agent makes a routing decision, a triage call, or an approval, the result is neither a conversation turn, a tool call, nor chain-of-thought reasoning. It is a discrete judgment output. These could be forced into "tool/action traces" (since a judgment call is technically a call), but the note's own description of that category emphasizes "sequences of external calls and their results," which reads as tool-use traces, not judgment outputs. The "at least" qualifier provides an escape hatch, but the taxonomy is presented as if it covers the interesting space without naming this gap.

- [Grounding — domain coverage] The note claims "This is the return-value problem from the scoping note in architectural form." The scoping note (llm-context-is-composed-without-scoping.md) discusses the return-value problem specifically as a question about what a sub-agent *returns* — framed through typing, codification, and interface contracts at frame boundaries. The session-history note uses "return-value problem" to mean something broader: the question of what any execution boundary should pass forward, including the scheduler's own loading decisions, not just sub-agent returns. The scoping note's return-value problem is about the sub-agent-to-parent interface; this note extends it to the scheduler-to-next-call interface, which is a different architectural relationship (the scheduler assembles context, it doesn't "call" itself). The mapping is plausible but the equation ("is ... in architectural form") overstates the alignment.

- [Grounding — Slate attribution] The note states: "From the public description, workers return episodes — compressed representations of what happened during a bounded action. These are not raw transcripts, so they fit the anti-transcript argument." The Slate ingest (slate-moving-beyond-react-and-rlm.ingest.md) confirms that episodes are compressed, but the ingest's own Limitations section warns: "Compression quality is assumed, not demonstrated. The entire architecture depends on episodes being good enough summaries... No evidence is provided that their compression is better." The note does acknowledge uncertainty about episode policy ("What we cannot tell from public evidence is the policy around episodes"), but the earlier sentence "they fit the anti-transcript argument" treats compression quality as sufficient to support the note's claim, when the source itself flags this as unvalidated. The note's hedging is in the right place but the affirmative sentence preceding it makes an unhedged claim that the source does not support.

INFO:
- [Completeness] The note lists five consequences of transcript inheritance breaking down (lines 42-46). Boundary case: **cost/latency scaling**. As traces accumulate, the literal token count and API cost increase. This is arguably implicit in "context pollution compounds," but it is a distinct practical concern (financial cost, latency) from the semantic concerns the other four items address. The note frames the breakdown primarily in information-quality terms rather than resource terms, though the bounded-context model it builds on treats cost as a first-class concern.

- [Completeness] The three coordination primitives in the "Conversation vs refinement" section (conversation, prompt refinement, context cloning/forking) are attributed to the conversation-vs-refinement note. Boundary case: **structured return values** — a sub-agent returns a typed artifact (JSON, a structured claim) that is neither a conversation trace, a refined prompt, nor a forked context. This is arguably covered by the note's broader "artifact-first loading" concept, but it does not appear in the enumerated list of coordination primitives. Since the enumeration is explicitly scoped to the conversation-vs-refinement note's local taxonomy, this may be intentional omission rather than a gap.

- [Internal consistency] The practical principle section says "move toward artifact-first loading once the caller's real consumption pattern is understood — but 'artifact-first' does not mean 'minimal'; a compressed episode that also serves memory and learning is still an artifact, not a transcript." This partially rehabilitates Slate's episode model. But the Tension section (lines 67-70) frames episodes as ambiguous: "more trace-shaped than a narrow result like yes/no or found X." The two sections are not contradictory, but the note's stance on episodes shifts from "ambiguous and possibly too trace-shaped" to "still an artifact, not a transcript" without explicitly reconciling. A reader could take either section as the note's settled position.

- [Completeness] The note's practical principle includes "use trace-preserving storage early, when you do not yet know the right interface or what later learning may need." Boundary case: **production systems with known interfaces that still keep full traces for compliance or audit**. The note's framing positions trace-preserving storage as an early/exploratory choice, but many mature systems preserve full traces not because they haven't designed better interfaces, but because regulatory or debugging requirements demand it. The note's "keep the raw trace as an auxiliary substrate" bullet partially covers this, but the sequential framing (early: traces; later: artifacts) could mislead readers into thinking trace storage is only an immaturity signal.

PASS:
- [Grounding — bounded-context orchestration model] The note's central framing — storage in K is cheap, bounded context is expensive, and `select(K)` is the control point — accurately reflects the bounded-context orchestration model note. The orchestration model explicitly states "accumulated state lives there, not in conversation history; LLM calls do judgment work and return results to code; the next prompt is assembled from stored state rather than from the model's memory of prior turns." The session-history note's argument is a direct application of this principle.

- [Grounding — chat-history note] The note attributes "The chat-history model trades context efficiency for implementation simplicity" and the "exploratory default" framing to the linked chat-history note. The chat-history note confirms both claims verbatim: "Chat is a strong exploratory default" and the title itself encodes the tradeoff claim. The session-history note's use of this source is accurate.

- [Grounding — scoping note sub-agents] The note's claim that "sub-agents should expose only return values across frames, not internal conversations" aligns with the scoping note, which states: "A sub-agent gets a fresh context — its own system prompt, its own input, no inherited conversation history. The parent sees only the return value, not the internal reasoning." Attribution is accurate.

- [Grounding — distillation note] The note links to distillation.md for "execution-boundary compression." The distillation note defines distillation as "compressing knowledge so that a consumer can act on it within bounded context." Framing boundary compression as distillation is a valid application — the execution boundary is a natural place where source material is compressed for the next consumer. The link relationship ("mechanism") is appropriate.

- [Internal consistency — store vs. load separation] The note's central claim ("the key separation is not 'store vs discard' but 'persist in symbolic state vs load into bounded context'") is maintained consistently throughout. The practical principle, the trace-type taxonomy, the Slate tension section, and the failure-handling paragraph all reinforce this same distinction without contradicting it.

- [Internal consistency — title and body alignment] The title claim ("Session history should not be the default next context") is faithfully represented by the body. The body does not argue that session history should never be loaded, only that it should not be the *default*. The practical principle section explicitly preserves room for trace-preserving approaches early in a design, consistent with the "default" framing in the title.

Overall: 3 warnings, 4 info

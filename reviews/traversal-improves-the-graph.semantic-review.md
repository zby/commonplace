- [Grounding alignment — domain coverage] The note attributes the "incremental formalization" insight to arscontexta (line 11) and then uses it to ground the claim that traversals are "read-write opportunities." But the arscontexta related-systems review describes arscontexta's model as one where agents improve notes *in place during traversal* — which is the opposite of what this note recommends (deferred improvement via log). The insight being borrowed is arguably just "traversal can improve the graph," which is generic enough to be uncontroversial; the actual design contribution of this note (deferred logging) has no grounding in arscontexta at all. Readers may over-attribute the deferred-logging design to arscontexta's methodology.

- [Internal consistency] Claim 9 states the log entry is "the lowest-friction capture possible." Strictly, doing nothing is lower friction than appending a line. The claim likely means "lowest-friction capture that preserves the signal," but as written it's an absolute that could be challenged. Minor precision issue.

- [Grounding alignment — wikiwiki principle] The note says the log pattern "matches the wikiwiki principle: capture with zero friction, refine later" (claim 8). The wikiwiki note describes progressive refinement *in place* — "it grows structure in place" — and explicitly says "No migration, no new file, no pipeline." A log entry that gets processed into edits across other files is a pipeline, not in-place refinement. The structural analogy holds at a high level (low-friction capture first, structure later), but the wikiwiki note's emphasis on in-place refinement is specifically what this note's design avoids. The attribution is reasonable but readers familiar with the wikiwiki note might see tension.

=== SEMANTIC REVIEW: epiplexity-eli5.md ===

Claims identified: 14

1. [Title/Lede] "Entropy asks how surprising the symbols are. Complexity asks how short the best exact recipe is. Epiplexity asks how much structure *this observer* can actually extract with the tools and time they have."
2. [Four measures table] The four measures are information content, Shannon entropy, Kolmogorov complexity, and epiplexity — and the first three do not depend on a practical observer once formal setup is fixed.
3. [Four measures table] Epiplexity is calculated as "area under a bounded learner's loss curve above final loss."
4. [Warm-up] For `ABABABABABABABAB` under a naive i.i.d. model, I = 16 bits.
5. [Warm-up] Kolmogorov complexity of `ABABABABABABABAB` is ~30 bytes of code for a 16-byte string.
6. [Warm-up] Epiplexity of the AB sequence for a bounded learner is approximately 2.6 bits.
7. [Warm-up] For a truly random binary sequence, epiplexity is 0 because loss stays flat and area above final loss is zero.
8. [Example 1] AES-encrypted patterned message has low Kolmogorov complexity "if the plaintext and key have short descriptions."
9. [Example 1] "Neither entropy nor complexity can express 'Bob can read it but Eve cannot.' Epiplexity can."
10. [Example 2] Ordered and shuffled textbooks have "nearly identical" Kolmogorov complexity.
11. [Example 2] "This is why distillation creates value: rearranging and compressing knowledge can raise epiplexity for the target reader even while reducing token count."
12. [Example 3] CSPRNG output has "still near zero" epiplexity even with the seed, because "regenerating the bytes does not reveal hidden meaning."
13. [Example 4] Chess notation has four levels of epiplexity depending on observer (zero / low / moderate / high).
14. [The pattern] "Epiplexity fills that gap: structure that is both present in the data and accessible to the observer within their budget."

---

WARN:
- [Completeness] The four-measures table claims epiplexity requires "a learner, its tools, and a compute budget," but the note's own examples repeatedly define the observer as including side information (encryption keys, decompressors, domain expertise) that are not captured by the phrase "compute budget." The Finzi et al. paper defines epiplexity in terms of a model class with bounded computation time. The note silently extends "bounded computation" to encompass possession of cryptographic keys (Example 1) and domain knowledge (Example 4). These are different kinds of observer-dependence: a key is not more compute, it is side information. The note's own locked-box framing ("intelligence, side information, prior knowledge, keys, decompressors, domain expertise, and time") is more accurate than the table. The table's "compute budget" column understates what epiplexity (as the note uses it) actually depends on, and a reader who trusts the table will be surprised when the examples invoke non-computational observer properties.

- [Grounding] The note says Kolmogorov complexity of the encrypted message "can still be low if the plaintext and key have short descriptions." This is correct for K(ciphertext | key), but standard Kolmogorov complexity K(ciphertext) includes the key in the description. The note does not clarify whether it means conditional or unconditional K. If the key is a 128-bit random string, K(ciphertext) is at least ~128 bits (since the shortest program must embed the key), which is not obviously "low" for a 16-byte message (128 bits of content). The claim is technically defensible under certain readings but is stated without the qualification needed to make it precise.

- [Completeness] The note claims "Epiplexity" in the summary table's "What it misses" column says "Nothing — observer-dependence is the point." This is a scope claim that no information-relevant aspect is missed. But epiplexity (as defined by Finzi et al.) measures learnable structure from sequential data via prequential coding — it captures *pattern* extraction but not *fact-level* observer-relativity. The parent note (information-value-is-observer-relative.md) explicitly flags this gap in its Open Questions: "Observer-relativity applies to both patterns (require computational depth to extract) and facts (require prior knowledge to interpret). Are these the same phenomenon or two phenomena that share a surface shape?" And in its Relevant Notes section, it describes the paper as capturing "the pattern-extraction aspect" but not "fact-level observer-relativity." The ELI5 note's claim that epiplexity misses "nothing" is stronger than what the KB's own grounding note asserts.

INFO:
- [Grounding] The note says "Finzi et al. use CSPRNGs as the canonical example of zero-epiplexity data." The ingest confirms this (CSPRNGs have zero epiplexity for bounded observers is listed as a theoretical result). However, the note's Example 3 adds a claim not in the source: that CSPRNG output has "still near zero" epiplexity *even when the observer possesses the seed*. The note's reasoning ("regenerating the bytes does not reveal hidden meaning") is plausible — knowing the seed lets you reproduce the output but does not reveal learnable structure in it. But this specific claim (epiplexity with seed) is an inference from the note, not an attribution to Finzi et al. The distinction matters because an observer with the seed could predict the entire sequence perfectly, which means their loss curve drops to zero immediately — the area above final loss would then be approximately zero for a different reason (near-instant convergence rather than permanently flat loss). The note's reasoning ("does not reveal hidden meaning") papers over this subtlety.

- [Internal consistency] The warm-up section says "Epiplexity = area under the loss curve, above the final loss" and then computes approximately 2.6 bits. The calculation sums `(1-0) + (1-0) + (0.5-0) + (0.1-0) + ...` which is the total area, not the area normalized per symbol. This is consistent with the definition stated in the table ("area under a bounded learner's loss curve above final loss") but inconsistent with how the note later uses epiplexity as a comparative measure across examples of different lengths. A 16-symbol string and a 10,000-symbol string with the same pattern would have very different total areas but the same per-symbol epiplexity rate. The note never clarifies whether epiplexity is a total or a rate, and the examples work because they are qualitative comparisons (zero vs. nonzero), but a careful reader would notice the ambiguity.

- [Completeness] The chess notation example (Example 4) assigns "Zero" epiplexity to someone who does not know chess. This is reasonable as an intuitive claim but raises a boundary question: the symbols `1. e4 e5 2. Nf3 Nc6` have statistical regularities (letter-digit patterns, common bigrams) that a pattern-learner could extract even without understanding chess. A bounded learner would see *some* loss-curve improvement from surface-level regularities. The note's "zero" is colloquial (meaning "no meaningful chess knowledge extracted"), not literally zero in the prequential coding sense.

- [Grounding] The note links to distillation.md with the claim "This is why distillation creates value: rearranging and compressing knowledge can raise epiplexity for the target reader even while reducing token count." The distillation note defines distillation as "compressing knowledge so that a consumer can act on it within bounded context." The ELI5 note's claim is consistent with the distillation note and with the epiplexity ingest (which lists "deterministic transformations create information for bounded observers" as an extractable value). The inference chain is sound: the shuffled textbook example shows a deterministic reordering changing extractable structure, and distillation includes reordering as one of its operations. However, the shuffled-textbook example demonstrates *arrangement* specifically, while distillation also includes *selection* and *compression* — the note treats the example as fully illustrative of distillation when it only covers one dimension.

PASS:
- [Internal consistency] The four measures are used consistently across all four examples and the summary table. Each example holds three measures constant while varying the fourth (or varies the observer while holding all four's inputs constant). No contradictions found between sections.
- [Internal consistency] The note's definition of epiplexity as "area under loss curve above final loss" is used consistently in every example. The warm-up shows the calculation explicitly; Examples 1-4 apply it qualitatively. No definition drift.
- [Grounding] The note's central framing — that entropy and complexity are observer-independent while epiplexity is observer-dependent — aligns with the Finzi et al. ingest, which states the paper "separates information into two components: time-bounded entropy (irreducible randomness given computational constraints) and epiplexity (learnable structural patterns visible within those constraints)."
- [Grounding] The link to reverse-compression.md is sound. The ELI5 note claims expanding text without adding extractable structure is a failure mode. The reverse-compression note defines exactly this: "expanding a compact signal into verbose prose without adding extractable structure."
- [Grounding] The link to context-efficiency.md is sound. The ELI5 note claims "identical tokens can differ in usable structure depending on observer and arrangement." The context-efficiency note's argument about volume vs. complexity dimensions supports this — same tokens, different usability depending on arrangement and observer capacity.
- [Completeness] The warm-up section's walk-through of all four measures for a single string is pedagogically complete — it covers each measure, shows model-dependence for information content and entropy, program-length for K, and the loss-curve computation for epiplexity.

Overall: 3 warnings, 4 info

WARN:
- [Completeness] The note claims Kirsch's four barriers "share a deeper structure" unified by reach, and demonstrates this by re-describing each barrier through a reach lens. But the unification is one-directional: it shows that reach is *present* in each barrier, not that reach is what *distinguishes* them from non-barriers. A counter-probe: a one-off data analysis script also embeds operational knowledge with some reach (the column-joining logic might be reusable next quarter), yet the note classifies this as low-reach. The note's own example ("which columns to join, how to handle nulls — that is specific to this query") asserts low reach by stipulation rather than by applying a test. The note lacks a mechanism for *measuring* reach, which means the framework risks being unfalsifiable — any case can be classified post-hoc as high- or low-reach to match the desired conclusion. Kirsch's original barriers at least name observable structural properties (state, integration surfaces, etc.); the reach reframing trades those observables for a more abstract concept without supplying an operationalization.

- [Grounding alignment — scope mismatch] The note uses Deutsch's "reach" concept (via the linked first-principles-reasoning note) as the unifying vocabulary. In the source note, reach is a property of *explanatory* knowledge: "explanatory knowledge applies beyond its original context because the explanation captures structure that isn't context-dependent." The reviewed note applies reach to *operational* knowledge — edge-case fixes, migration constraints, API shapes, audit resolutions. These are closer to what Deutsch would call adaptive information (structures that help a system cope) than explanatory knowledge (theories about why things work). The note implicitly broadens "reach" from a property of explanations to a property of any resolution that transfers, without flagging this as a vocabulary extension. A reader following the link to the first-principles note may expect Deutsch's narrower sense.

- [Completeness — boundary zone] The three-zone taxonomy (low-reach, boundary, high-reach) is presented as a "cleaner prediction," but the boundary zone definition — "tools that start local but pick up users, persistent state, integrations, or compliance obligations" — is essentially a restatement of Kirsch's original barriers (state, integrations, interface stability, auditability) rather than a prediction derived from reach. The reach framing is supposed to improve on listing barriers, but the boundary zone reverts to listing the same barriers. This undermines the claim that reach provides a "cleaner prediction" than Kirsch's original categories.

INFO:
- [Grounding alignment — vocabulary] The note says "Kirsch's durable artifact stack is therefore not sentimental attachment to code. It is a way of storing high-reach operational knowledge in a form future runs, humans, and systems can inherit." Kirsch himself does not use the concept of "reach" or frame his argument in those terms. His argument is about concrete engineering bottlenecks (validation, state, interfaces, auditability). The reach reinterpretation is the note's own analytical move, which is legitimate, but the phrasing "Kirsch's durable artifact stack is therefore..." could read as attributing the reach framing to Kirsch rather than applying it to his work.

- [Grounding alignment — domain coverage] The link to the ephemeral-computation-prevents-accumulation note is used to support the claim that "accumulation is only worth its overhead when what you'd accumulate has reach." The linked note actually makes a broader point: accumulation enables learning across runs, testing, review, and reuse — none of which are contingent on reach specifically. The ephemeral-computation note's framework is about the *structural consequences* of discarding vs. persisting artifacts, not about whether those artifacts have reach. Reach is this note's additional criterion layered on top, not something the foundation note provides.

- [Internal consistency — "what this framing does and doesn't explain"] The final section acknowledges that the reach framing "predicts where durability pressure appears but not which artifact should carry the knowledge" and that "AI may expand the low-reach zone." This is an appropriate scope limitation. However, it sits in mild tension with the earlier strong claim that "the reach of the embedded knowledge, not the complexity of the code, is what makes ephemerality unsafe" — because if AI can make medium-reach knowledge cheaper to re-derive, then reach alone does not determine safety; re-derivation cost also matters. The note gestures at this ("relocates the durable substrate") but does not reconcile it with the title claim, which presents reach as the sole determining variable.

- [Completeness — missing boundary case] Consider operational knowledge that has high reach but is fully captured in a declarative specification (e.g., a formal schema definition, a regulatory rule encoded in a config file). In such cases, the knowledge has high reach, but the *code* implementing it might be safely ephemeral because the durable substrate is the spec, not the implementation. The note's framework does not clearly distinguish between reach of the knowledge and reach of the specific artifact form. The "What this framing does and doesn't explain" section acknowledges this gap implicitly ("not which artifact should carry the knowledge") but the earlier sections treat "embedded operational knowledge" and the software artifact as co-extensive.

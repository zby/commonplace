=== SEMANTIC REVIEW: elicitation-requires-maintained-question-generation-systems.md ===

Claims identified: 14

1. "The user who needs the model's knowledge most is least equipped to cue it — the expertise gap makes the activation problem self-reinforcing." (Opening paragraph)
2. "Viable strategies must either externalize expert knowledge into the workflow or use general-purpose probes that activate domain-specific reasoning without requiring domain-specific questions." (Opening paragraph)
3. Four strategies ordered from most to least expertise required: direct targeted probes, perspective assignments, domain checklists, structured adversarial prompts. (Section: Strategies ordered by expertise required)
4. "Activation is near-perfect [for direct targeted probes], but only because the user already knows what to ask." (Strategy 1)
5. "McConnell's Code Complete found that perspective-assigned reviewers uncover more defects than undirected review" — attributed to McConnell, applied to LLMs by analogy. (Strategy 2)
6. "The user needs to know which perspectives matter, not which specific problems to look for. This reduces the expertise requirement from 'know the failure mode' to 'know the stakeholder.'" (Strategy 2)
7. "Checklists ossify — they cover known failure families but not novel ones." (Strategy 3)
8. "[Structured adversarial prompts have] the lowest expertise barrier among the probe types." (Strategy 4)
9. Two-pass review architecture: unconstrained self-review (pass 1) + mandatory probes from strategies 2-4 (pass 2). (Section: Composing strategies)
10. "The second pass constructs the oracle the first pass fails to be." (Section: Composing strategies)
11. Strategies 1-4 address known unknowns; unknown unknowns require question-generating mechanisms. (Section: Beyond known failure families)
12. Five-step checklist lifecycle: seed, enforce, log misses, distill updates, prune stale probes. (Section: Checklist lifecycle)
13. Activation gap measurement via probed vs. open conditions. (Section: Measurement)
14. Three system health metrics: question coverage, discovery rate, probe staleness rate. (Section: Measurement)

WARN:
- [Completeness] The four-strategy ordering claims to be ordered by "expertise required of the user," but the boundary between strategies 2 (perspective assignments) and 4 (structured adversarial prompts) is blurry. "What happens with 1,000 concurrent users?" is listed as an example of perspective assignment (strategy 2), but it fits equally well as a structured adversarial prompt (strategy 4) — it is a parameterized template ("what happens at Nx scale?") that does not require domain-specific knowledge. The note itself uses this example under strategy 2, yet the structure of the question matches strategy 4's definition. If the same concrete probe can be classified as either strategy 2 or strategy 4, the taxonomy has an overlap that the ordering does not acknowledge.
- [Grounding] The note says "McConnell's Code Complete found that perspective-assigned reviewers uncover more defects than undirected review — the same principle applies to LLMs, where the role assignment shapes which stored knowledge gets activated." The source (the-bug-that-shipped) references McConnell but attributes a narrower claim: "assigning reviewers specific perspectives... can uncover more defects than general review." The note's extension to LLMs ("the same principle applies") is the note's own inference, not McConnell's or the source's finding. The source demonstrates that targeted questions get better answers than open review, but it does not test perspective assignment as a distinct mechanism — it tests direct targeted probes ("What happens if 1,000 clients hit this simultaneously?"). The note conflates the source's evidence for strategy 1 (direct targeted probes) with support for strategy 2 (perspective assignments).
- [Completeness] The five-step checklist lifecycle (seed, enforce, log misses, distill updates, prune stale probes) is presented as a complete maintenance loop. A boundary case: what about re-prioritization or re-ordering of probes? As the set grows, the context budget becomes a constraint — not every probe can be included in every review. The lifecycle includes "prune stale probes" but does not address selection or triage among active probes when the set exceeds what fits in a single pass. This is a missing step between enforce and prune that matters at scale.

INFO:
- [Completeness] The four strategies are implicitly scoped to probe-based elicitation — all four involve formulating questions or perspectives. A boundary case: environmental or architectural elicitation, where the workflow structure itself activates knowledge (e.g., requiring the model to produce a deployment checklist before marking a task complete, or routing code through a second model with a different system prompt). These are not probes in the sense the note describes — they are structural constraints that force activation. The note's "two-pass review" section gestures toward this but does not name it as a distinct strategy class. This may be intentional scoping rather than a gap, but it is worth flagging because the title claim ("elicitation requires maintained question-generation systems") implies question generation is the only mechanism, when structural forcing is an alternative.
- [Grounding] The link to professional-software-developers-dont-vibe-they-control is described as "complement: expert practitioners compensate for activation limits through explicit planning, narrow tasking, and supervision — a human version of two-pass review." The source (Huang et al.) does describe experienced developers controlling agent behavior through planning and supervision, but does not frame this as compensating for "activation limits" — it frames it as maintaining software quality attributes. The note's reinterpretation through the activation lens is reasonable but is the note's own framing, not the source's.
- [Grounding] The link to towards-a-science-of-ai-agent-reliability is described as "context: reliability dimensions motivate the measurement framework." The connection is plausible — Rabanser et al.'s predictability dimension (calibration vs. discrimination) is conceptually adjacent to the activation gap measurement. However, the note's measurement framework (activation gap, question coverage, discovery rate, probe staleness) does not directly derive from Rabanser et al.'s four dimensions. The link overstates the grounding relationship; "inspired by" would be more accurate than "motivate."
- [Internal consistency] The note says structured adversarial prompts have "the lowest expertise barrier among the probe types" (strategy 4), but the two-pass review section says pass 2 uses "mandatory probes from strategies 2-4." If strategy 4 has the lowest barrier, it should be the default for users who cannot construct perspective assignments (strategy 2) or domain checklists (strategy 3). The composability claim implicitly assumes all three are available, which requires at least checklist-author expertise (strategy 3) and stakeholder-identification expertise (strategy 2). The note does not address what happens when only strategy 4 is available — is a single-strategy second pass still effective?

PASS:
- [Internal consistency] The core narrative is coherent: the activation gap (from the foundation note) creates a need for external question generation; four strategies address this at decreasing expertise levels; strategies compose into a two-pass architecture; the architecture needs a maintenance loop to prevent ossification. Each section builds on the previous without contradiction.
- [Grounding] The link to knowledge-storage-does-not-imply-contextual-activation accurately represents that note's claims. The opening paragraph's "since knowledge storage does not imply contextual activation" correctly summarizes the foundation note's central distinction between stored and activated knowledge, and the expertise gap is faithfully described.
- [Grounding] The link to the-bug-that-shipped accurately represents the source's core finding: models retrieve deployment-failure knowledge on direct probe (100%) but rarely surface it in undirected self-review (13% aggregate). The note's use of this as motivating evidence for structured elicitation is well-grounded.
- [Grounding] The link to oracle-strength-spectrum is accurately described: "elicitation strategies are oracle-hardening moves for activation-limited settings." The oracle-strength note's framework (manufacture, amplify, monitor) maps naturally to the elicitation note's checklist lifecycle (seed, enforce, log, distill, prune).
- [Internal consistency] The "known unknowns vs. unknown unknowns" distinction in "Beyond known failure families" is consistent with the strategies section — the note correctly identifies that all four strategies address known failure families and that a separate mechanism (incident analysis, cross-model disagreement, etc.) is needed to discover new ones.
- [Completeness] The expertise ordering of the four strategies holds under boundary testing. Direct targeted probes clearly require the most expertise (you must know the exact failure). Domain checklists clearly externalize that expertise. Structured adversarial prompts ("what breaks at 100x scale?") require only generic scaling intuition. The ordering is sound even if the boundary between 2 and 4 is fuzzy (see WARN above).

WARN:
- [Confidence miscalibration] The note presents its reframing as established fact rather than a proposed analytical framework. "This reframes iteration length as an **interpretation-error propagation bound**" is the note's own construction, but the surrounding prose asserts it directly: "short iterations limit how far a wrong interpretation can propagate before it's caught" and "The cost of correction is proportional to how much was built on the wrong reading." These are plausible but ungrounded claims — no empirical evidence is cited for the proportionality claim or for the assertion that disambiguation failure is a major component of what agile calls requirement change. The Open Questions section acknowledges this ("What fraction of 'requirement changes' in real agile projects are genuine change vs disambiguation failures? Is there empirical data?"), but the body reads as if the answer is already known.
  Recommendation: Hedge the body to match its actual epistemic status. E.g., "short iterations would plausibly limit how far a wrong interpretation can propagate" and "the cost of correction is likely proportional to..." — or alternatively, add grounding evidence that justifies direct assertion.

- [Proportion mismatch] The note's core claim is the conflation identified in the title — that "changing requirements" hides two distinct phenomena. The section that carries this claim ("Two kinds of 'changing requirements'") is the shortest substantive section at roughly 80 words of body text. Meanwhile "Implications for deploy-time learning" and "The cascading interpretation problem" each receive comparable or greater development. The deploy-time learning section in particular extends the claim into a specific application domain rather than developing the core distinction itself — the distinction is stated and then the note moves on to implications before fully establishing why it matters.
  Recommendation: Develop the "Two kinds" section further — perhaps with a concrete example showing the same observable symptom (team changes direction mid-sprint) diagnosed both ways. Consider whether the deploy-time learning section belongs in a separate note or should be shortened to a linking paragraph.

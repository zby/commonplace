=== SEMANTIC REVIEW: trace-derived-learning-techniques-in-related-systems.md ===

Claims identified: 16

WARN:
- [Completeness] The five recurring stages claim ("the same five stages appear") omits a stage present in several surveyed systems: **deduplication/conflict resolution**. cass-memory has Jaccard similarity deduplication and conflict detection; ExpeL and G-Memory have explicit EDIT/REMOVE/MERGE operations that resolve conflicts between new extractions and existing artifacts; ClawVault's observer deduplicates scored observations. These are not extraction (stage 3) or promotion (stage 4) — they sit between them, deciding how a new candidate relates to existing knowledge. The note's own "What looks borrowable" section calls out "explicit mutation verbs on learned artifacts" as a notable feature, which implicitly acknowledges this sixth concern without folding it into the five-stage framework.

- [Completeness] The four-category Axis 1 taxonomy (single-session extension, cross-agent aggregator, service-owned backend, trajectory-run pattern) is presented as exhaustive ("the systems separate on two axes"), but the boundary between "service-owned trace backend" and "trajectory-run pattern" is strained for Autocontext. Autocontext owns its trace format (SQLite, competitor outputs, playbooks) like a service backend, but learns from repeated runs like a trajectory system. The note classifies it under trajectory-run, yet its description — "run-trajectory mining into playbooks, session reports, JSONL training exports" — equally fits a self-owned trace backend that happens to iterate. The taxonomy handles this by assigning Autocontext to trajectory-run, but the fit requires knowing that "trajectory-run" means "learns from repeated episodes" rather than "consumes trajectories," which would also describe the service backends.

- [Grounding] The note's concluding claim — "session- or trajectory-derived candidate generation is a solved enough pattern to copy; oracle-backed evaluation is not" — is attributed as "the concrete update to automating KB learning is an open problem." The linked note does discuss the oracle gap extensively, but the specific formulation "solved enough pattern to copy" is the survey note's own editorial upgrade. The automating-KB-learning note says "the extraction side of the loop is now concrete in source code" (in its backlink to this note), which is weaker than "solved enough to copy." The inference is reasonable but readers could mistake the bold sentence as grounded in the linked note rather than being this note's own synthesis.

- [Completeness] The "What remains open" section claims "none of the fifteen systems demonstrates principled retirement, cross-domain abstraction, or explanatory-reach judgment in open-ended settings." cass-memory's `invertToAntiPattern()` mechanism — where harmful feedback exceeding a threshold converts a bullet into an "AVOID:" anti-pattern — and its maturity progression to `deprecated` status based on effective score are at least a mechanical form of principled retirement. The note's own cass-memory section describes this (maturity progresses "candidate → established → proven → deprecated based on effective score"). The "What remains open" claim may be using "principled" to mean something stronger than score-based deprecation, but that distinction is not made explicit, creating tension with the detailed cass-memory description earlier in the note.

INFO:
- [Completeness] The artifact structure spectrum (six levels from "minimal verbal hints" to "executable code") is presented as a range, not an exhaustive taxonomy, which is appropriate. However, one boundary case worth noting: **structured database records with graph relationships** (G-Memory's Chroma-backed episode store plus task-similarity graph) sit awkwardly between "scored flat rules" and "structured records." G-Memory appears in both categories — scored insights and episode retrieval — suggesting the spectrum conflates artifact granularity with artifact structure. The note partially acknowledges this by listing G-Memory under "scored flat rules" for its insights while separately describing its three-substrate architecture.

- [Completeness] The "uniqueness" claims — "the only inspected system" for cass-memory (cross-agent), OpenViking (shared service), Autocontext (bridging both targets), Voyager (executable artifacts), G-Memory (multi-agent) — are each scoped to the fifteen inspected systems. These are accurate within scope but the source-only systems could challenge some. Trajectory-Informed Memory Generation could potentially be a second system bridging both artifact forms depending on its unknown implementation details. The note handles this correctly by noting the source-only systems "are included with lower confidence," but a reader skimming the uniqueness claims could miss the qualification.

- [Internal consistency] The note states Autocontext is "the clearest inspected system spanning both artifact learning and weight promotion" in its section heading context, then in Axis 2 says "Autocontext bridges both — symbolic artifacts first, then optionally weights." The word "optionally" is important: if weight promotion is optional, Autocontext is primarily an artifact-learning system that can also feed weight training. This is internally consistent but the emphasis differs between sections — the Autocontext section foregrounds the bridging role while the Axis 2 section hedges it.

- [Grounding] The link to "a functioning knowledge base needs a workshop layer, not just a library" is annotated "grounds the artifact-promotion side of this survey: several systems operationalize workshop-to-library bridges." The workshop note discusses the library/workshop distinction in terms of value trajectory (accumulating vs consumed), but the surveyed systems' artifact promotion is not always workshop-to-library in that note's sense. Reflexion's rolling buffer and Dynamic Cheatsheet's rewrite are ephemeral working state, not durable library artifacts — they are more like workshop documents that never graduate. The grounding relationship is real for systems like cass-memory and Napkin but overstated for the ephemeral-artifact systems.

- [Internal consistency] The table in "Log formats matter more than the prompts" lists 17 systems (15 code-inspected plus AgeMem and Trajectory-Informed), matching the count in the introduction. However, the "What looks borrowable" section draws lessons only from the code-inspected systems, which is appropriate but unstated. The source-only systems could contribute borrowable patterns (AgeMem's curriculum structure is discussed in the oracle-dependent note as a borrowable insight) but are silently excluded here.

PASS:
- [Grounding] The link to "learning substrates, backends, and artifact forms" is accurately used. The survey note says "service memory is usually a backend choice within the symbolic artifact substrate, not a third top-level substrate alongside artifacts and weights," and the linked note confirms this: "Repo-hosted markdown is one important backend, especially for commonplace, but it is not the whole symbolic artifact substrate." The vocabulary (substrate class, backend, artifact form) is used consistently between the two notes.

- [Grounding] The link to "continuous learning requires durability, not weight updates" is accurately annotated: "the survey's non-weight cases count as continuous learning because their improvements persist." The linked note's central claim — "continuous learning requires durable adaptive change, not specifically weight change" — directly supports the survey's treatment of artifact-learning systems as genuine learning systems.

- [Grounding] The link to "automating KB learning is an open problem" accurately represents the relationship. The survey note says extraction is concrete while evaluation remains open; the linked note's own "Open problems" section identifies evaluation as the primary gap, and the linked note's backlink to this survey says "the extraction side of the loop is now concrete in source code."

- [Internal consistency] The two-axis framework (ingestion pattern x promotion target) is applied consistently across all seventeen systems. Each system maps to exactly one ingestion category and one or two promotion targets, with Autocontext as the explicitly noted bridge case. No system is left unclassified.

- [Internal consistency] The per-system descriptions consistently follow the five-stage structure (Trigger, Source format, Extraction, Promotion, Scope), with minor variations in section naming (some include Reinjection explicitly, others fold it into Promotion or Scope). The structure is stable enough to support cross-system comparison.

- [Completeness] The boundary case of "the simplest possible instance" — a system that merely logs traces without any extraction or promotion — is handled by the note's framework: such a system would have stages 1-2 but not 3-5, placing it outside the survey's scope. The note's framing ("all learn from traces") correctly excludes pure logging.

Overall: 4 warnings, 5 info

WARN:
- [Completeness] The note claims "accumulation" is "the most basic" learning operation and names it alongside the scope table, but the note also uses "discovery," "constraining," and "distillation" as operations elsewhere. The note never enumerates the full set of learning operations; the word "most basic" implies a hierarchy of operations, but the hierarchy is only partially visible. A reader could mistake accumulation as the *only* operation discussed under generality, when in fact the scope table mixes accumulation (adding a typo fix, adding a connection) with what might be constraining or methodology improvement. The table rows do not all map cleanly to "accumulation" — "Define structured sections for a type" and "Improve methodology" look more like constraining or discovery than accumulation. If accumulation means "adding knowledge to the store," then methodology improvement is not accumulation of a stored item but a change to how the system operates. The note's own framework strains here.

- [Completeness] The compound is defined as reliability, speed, and cost, which "tend to move together." Boundary case: there are capacity improvements where these three dimensions diverge rather than co-move. Adding extensive validation checks improves reliability but *decreases* speed and *increases* cost. The note acknowledges the compound "isn't exclusive to codification" and gives conventions/caching/distilled-skills as examples, but some of those improve only one dimension (caching improves speed, not necessarily reliability or cost). The claim that the three "often improve simultaneously" may overstate the co-movement for non-codification cases. The compound framing works well for codification but becomes strained for the softer cases the note lists.

- [Grounding — domain coverage] The note uses Deutsch's "reach" concept (via the linked first-principles-reasoning note) to partition the generality axis into facts vs theories. But the first-principles note frames reach as a property of *explanatory* knowledge — knowledge that says *why* something works. The note under review maps reach onto a continuous spectrum from "Fix a typo" to "Improve methodology," treating it as a gradient. The source note treats adaptive vs explanatory as a qualitative distinction ("two kinds of knowledge that mainstream usage conflates"), not a gradient. The note's scope table presents a smooth continuum where Deutsch's framework posits a qualitative break. This is a reasonable extension but could mislead readers into thinking Deutsch endorses a smooth spectrum.

INFO:
- [Completeness] The "Other dimensions" section introduces composability as a capacity dimension distinct from generality and the compound, but the section is brief and leaves the relationship underspecified. Composability ("capacity to learn further") could be reframed as a special case of generality (a verified claim is more *generally* useful as a premise) or as a form of reliability (verified claims are more *reliable* as premises). The note acknowledges these dimensions "don't exhaust what matters" but doesn't clarify whether composability is truly orthogonal to the two main axes or a second-order effect of them.

- [Grounding — attribution] The note says "Argyris's single-loop vs double-loop learning maps onto this axis as rough regions." The qualifier "rough regions" is appropriate, but the mapping elides a key difference: Argyris's framework is about *organizational* learning and governing variables (norms, strategies, assumptions), while the note's generality axis is about scope of applicability. Single-loop/double-loop is about *what level of the system changes*, not *how broadly the change applies*. A double-loop change could be narrow in scope (changing one team's governing assumption) while still being double-loop. The mapping is suggestive but the domains don't align precisely.

- [Internal consistency] The note says "the generality-vs-compound trade-off is the primary dynamic" and links to the constraining-and-distillation note, which describes how both mechanisms trade generality for compound. But the note also says "Learning cuts across Argyris's loops — it can be single-loop (codifying one check into a script) or double-loop (deciding that claim notes should use Toulmin-derived sections)." The codification example is a compound gain (reliability, speed, cost). The Toulmin-derived sections example looks more like a generality gain (a design principle applicable to all future claim notes). If learning "cuts across" loops, and both loops can involve either generality or compound gains, then the generality-vs-compound distinction is orthogonal to Argyris's loops rather than "mapping onto" them. The note seems to want it both ways — Argyris maps onto the generality axis (earlier claim) and also cuts across both axes (later claim). These are compatible if "maps onto as rough regions" is loose enough, but the tension is worth noting.

- [Completeness] Boundary case: *forgetting* or *pruning*. Simon's definition covers "any change" in capacity, including decreases. The note focuses entirely on capacity increases (learning as improvement). Deliberate forgetting — retiring obsolete facts, pruning dead connections — is also a capacity change that can improve the system's adaptation. The scope table has no row for removal. This is not necessarily a gap in the note's argument (the title claim is about dimensions of improvement, not about all capacity changes), but under Simon's definition as cited, a complete account of learning would include capacity changes through removal.

PASS:
- [Grounding — Simon] The Simon definition is quoted and used faithfully. The note correctly applies it: "By this definition, almost every KB improvement is learning." The definition is broad enough to cover the note's argument, and the note does not overstate what Simon claims.
- [Grounding — AgeMem] The claim that "AgeMem's 23-49% improvement on task completion comes entirely from storing and retrieving the right facts" aligns with the AgeMem note, which confirms that AgeMem's LTM Add stores facts (adaptive knowledge at the low-reach end) and the improvement comes from learning when to store and retrieve. The attribution is accurate.
- [Grounding — codification] The claim that codification "improves all three at once by changing the substrate" is confirmed by the codification note, which describes the medium change (markdown to Python) producing deterministic execution (reliability), faster execution (speed), and free execution (cost). Attribution is accurate.
- [Grounding — discovery] The claim that "Discovery — positing an abstraction and recognizing particulars as instances of it — is the operation that produces theories" accurately reflects the discovery note's dual structure: "You posit something general... you simultaneously recognize that things you already knew are instances of it." Attribution is accurate.
- [Internal consistency — title and body] The title claim ("learning is not only about generality") is faithfully developed: the body introduces the compound (reliability/speed/cost) as an equally valid dimension of capacity change, and the "Other dimensions" section acknowledges further axes. The note does not contradict its own title.
- [Internal consistency — definitions] The term "compound" is introduced with a clear definition ("reliability, speed, and cost... form a compound because they often improve simultaneously") and used consistently throughout. No definition drift detected.

Overall: 3 warnings, 4 info

=== SEMANTIC REVIEW: discovery-is-seeing-the-particular-as-an-instance-of-the-general.md ===

Claims identified: 16

WARN:
- [Completeness] The three-depths hierarchy (shared feature / shared structure / generative model) claims to be a depth ordering where "each level subsumes the previous." Boundary case: **analogical reasoning** — recognizing that electrical circuits and water pipes share relational structure (Gentner's structure mapping) is deeper than shared features but does not propose a generative model that *produces* both phenomena. It sits cleanly at "shared structure." However, **metaphor** — e.g., "argument is war" (Lakoff) — maps structure across domains in a way that shapes reasoning and generates predictions, yet does not propose a causal mechanism. It falls between shared structure and generative model: it has generative power (produces new inferences) but is not an "abstract machine that produces both phenomena." The hierarchy's middle level ("extract a common pattern") and top level ("propose an abstract machine") leave a gap for mappings that generate inferences without positing a mechanism. The note's own phrasing — "Explanatory — explains why the similarity exists and predicts new instances" — bundles explanation and prediction together, but productive metaphors predict without explaining.

- [Grounding] The note claims the three-depths hierarchy "draws on Alexander's levels of concreteness — from structural templates through generative processes to mutual reinforcement." The Alexander note describes three levels of a different kind: (1) patterns as document types (concrete structure), (2) generative processes vs master plans (emergent methodology), (3) centers strengthening centers (mutual reinforcement). These are levels of *architectural abstraction* in knowledge system design, not levels of *similarity depth*. The note maps them as: structural templates -> shared structure, generative processes -> generative model. But Alexander's "generative processes" are about *how good structure emerges incrementally*, not about *proposing an abstract machine that produces two phenomena*. The reuse is creative but the attribution ("draws on") overstates the derivation. The hierarchy is the note's own construction; the Alexander connection is suggestive, not foundational.

- [Grounding / Domain coverage] The link to "information value is observer-relative" is annotated "grounds: the recognition cost hierarchy maps to computational bounds on structure extraction." The information-value note does discuss extraction cost and bounded observers, and it does reference back to this discovery note: "Discovery cost is observer-relative but not easily optimized — the data from which a connection could be inferred is present before anyone sees it, but extracting the pattern requires computation that scales with abstraction depth." However, the information-value note's framework is about *observer-relative value* (prior knowledge, computational capacity, tools, goals). The discovery note's recognition cost hierarchy is about *depth of abstraction* (surface vs structural vs generative). These are related but not the same: recognition cost could be observer-relative (a domain expert recognizes structural similarity cheaply), but the discovery note presents it as an intrinsic property of depth levels ("surface similarity: cheap... structural similarity: expensive"), not as observer-relative. The grounding claim is partially valid (the information-value note mentions this note's hierarchy) but the relationship is more accurately "extends" or "consistent with" than "grounds," since the information-value framework would actually complicate the discovery note's cost hierarchy by making it observer-dependent rather than depth-dependent.

INFO:
- [Completeness] The "dual structure" claim — that discovery requires simultaneously positing the general and recognizing the particular as an instance — is presented as indecomposable ("can't be decomposed"). Boundary case: **incremental generalization** where an observer first notices a recurring pattern across several instances (recognition accumulates) and only later names it as a concept (positing follows). The note's examples (mathematician extracting a lemma, Darwin) are compatible with the co-arising framing, but many real discoveries involve a gradual accumulation phase before the "aha" moment. The note could be describing the logical structure of the completed discovery rather than its temporal process. If so, the claim is defensible but should be read as structural, not processual.

- [Completeness] The note explicitly scopes its hierarchy to "similarity-based connections" and names contrastive, causal, and temporal links as outside scope. Boundary case: **explanatory links** — "X explains Y" — can be similarity-based (if X and Y share generative structure) or causal (if X causes Y). The scope boundary between similarity and causation may be less clean than the parenthetical suggests, since the "generative model" level explicitly involves explaining "why the similarity exists," which borders on causal explanation.

- [Completeness] The recognition cost hierarchy claims surface similarity is "cheap" because "embeddings, keywords, filenames get you there." Boundary case: **misleading surface similarity** — two notes that share vocabulary but discuss fundamentally different phenomena (e.g., "cell" in biology vs "cell" in spreadsheets). Surface tools will flag these as similar, producing false positives. The hierarchy addresses cost of recognition but not cost of *discrimination* — telling true similarity from coincidental vocabulary overlap. This is the flip side of the "adjacency is not connection" distinction from the arscontexta note, which the discovery note already links.

- [Internal consistency] The note claims "the highest-value act isn't linking two notes that share a mechanism — it's creating a new note that names the mechanism." This is stated as a conclusion from the naming-amortizes-discovery-cost argument. But the argument only shows that naming reduces *future recognition cost*. It does not show that naming is higher value than linking, since linking also has value (connecting existing knowledge for traversal). The inference is plausible — naming has compounding returns while linking has local returns — but the note presents it as a direct consequence rather than an additional claim that needs its own support.

- [Grounding] The link to the arscontexta note is annotated "refines: the 'controlled disorder' claim is right about judgment-based linking but the topic-vs-mechanism framing is a false dichotomy." The arscontexta note's "controlled disorder" claim (from Luhmann via article #23) is about requiring each cross-topical link to carry a defensible reason. The discovery note's refinement — that the relevant axis is depth, not topic-vs-mechanism — is a reasonable reinterpretation. However, the arscontexta note does not itself use a "topic-vs-mechanism" framing; it uses "adjacency-vs-connection." The "topic-vs-mechanism" framing may come from a different source or from the discovery note's own reading of the conceptual landscape. The refinement target is slightly misattributed.

PASS:
- [Internal consistency] The three-depths hierarchy is internally consistent. The table's "Operation" and "Power" columns align: naming a surface similarity is descriptive, extracting a common pattern is structural, proposing an abstract machine is explanatory. The subsumption claim holds: a generative model implies shared structure which implies shared features. No section contradicts another.
- [Internal consistency] The "dual structure" framing in the opening is consistent with how discovery is used later: naming a mechanism (positing the general) and recognizing instances (seeing the particular). The concept is introduced and applied consistently throughout.
- [Grounding] The link to "minimum viable vocabulary" is annotated "grounds: MVV reframes 'naming amortizes discovery cost' as an optimization problem." The MVV note explicitly states: "Naming amortizes discovery cost. Discovery argues that the highest-value act in a knowledge system is creating a named concept — once a structure has a name, recognizing further instances becomes cheap." This is a faithful attribution. The MVV note does build an optimization frame on top of the discovery note's naming claim. The grounding relationship is genuine and accurately described.
- [Grounding] The link to the "Notes Without Reasons" ingest is annotated "extends: the adjacency-vs-connection distinction maps to recognition depth, not link kind." The ingest note confirms this connection explicitly in its connections section: "the adjacency-vs-connection distinction maps to this note's recognition depth hierarchy; embedding adjacency is 'shared feature' level, curated links require deeper recognition." Both notes agree on the mapping. The relationship is accurately described.
- [Scope] The note's explicit scope limitation — "This hierarchy covers similarity-based connections" with named exclusions (contrastive, causal, temporal) — is a genuine epistemic boundary, not a hedge. The note does not overreach into the excluded domains.

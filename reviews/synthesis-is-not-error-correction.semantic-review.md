=== SEMANTIC REVIEW: synthesis-is-not-error-correction.md ===

Claims identified: 17

WARN:
- [Completeness] The binary taxonomy (synthesis vs. voting) is presented as an exhaustive partition of "what to do with multiple agent outputs," but the note itself identifies at least one case that does not fit cleanly: adversarial debate (Kim et al.'s Decentralized topology, 7.8x). The note acknowledges this in Open Questions ("may be a third category"), but the Design Rule section states the rule as if it is complete: "Redundant calls... need voting" / "Complementary calls... need synthesis." If debate is a third category, the design rule has a gap — what aggregation operation do you use for calls that are neither purely redundant nor purely complementary but iteratively refining? The note's own data (7.8x for debate, between 17.2x synthesis and 4.4x centralized verification) suggests debate occupies a real middle ground that the binary rule does not address.

- [Completeness] The claim "There is no mechanism to detect that agent 2 contradicts agents 1 and 3" (The distinction, synthesis description) overstates the case. Synthesis can be implemented with contradiction-detection steps — a synthesizer that notices disagreement and flags or resolves it is still performing synthesis (merging into a combined output) rather than voting (selecting one answer). The note treats synthesis as if it must be naive merge-all-inputs, but real synthesis pipelines often include reconciliation logic. This conflates a particular (naive) implementation of synthesis with synthesis as a category.

- [Grounding] The note says Kim et al.'s Independent topology uses "synthesis-only coordination" and attributes this phrase to the paper. The ingest confirms the term appears in the paper's Appendix E. However, the note then claims "The paper doesn't detail the synthesis mechanism" while also asserting with confidence that "it's not voting — outputs are merged rather than compared for agreement." If the mechanism is not detailed, the assertion that it cannot involve any comparison is an inference, not a grounded attribution. The ingest's methodological limitations section supports this reading ("the paper mentions voting as a possible orchestrator aggregation method but never implements it"), but the note should acknowledge that the absence of mechanism detail means the characterization as pure merge is the note's interpretation, not the paper's explicit statement.

INFO:
- [Completeness] The design rule maps redundant calls to voting and complementary calls to synthesis, but does not address the mixed case: what if calls are partially redundant and partially complementary? For example, agents solving overlapping sub-problems where some portions of each output are unique contributions and others are redundant attempts at the same sub-task. The note's binary partition (redundant vs. complementary) may need a hybrid strategy — vote on the overlapping portions, synthesize the unique contributions. This is a boundary case the framework does not address.

- [Grounding] The note claims "MAKER achieves zero errors over a million LLM steps." The MAKER ingest confirms this for the 20-disk Towers of Hanoi benchmark specifically. The note does not qualify the domain — a reader could interpret this as a general capability rather than a result on a single benchmark with hard per-step oracles. The MAKER section of the note does explain the three design conditions (maximal decomposition, hard oracles, decorrelation), which implicitly scope the claim, but the unqualified statement in the opening paragraph is stronger than the evidence supports.

- [Internal consistency] The note says voting "deliberately throws away information — that's the point" and identifies this as why it corrects errors. But the Open Questions section asks "Can you combine the two? Decompose a task, have multiple agents attempt each sub-task (vote to select), then synthesize across sub-tasks." This is consistent rather than contradictory, but it introduces a tension: if the design rule says redundant calls need voting and complementary calls need synthesis, the combined approach requires the scheduler to know which calls are redundant and which are complementary before choosing the aggregation operation. The note does not address who makes this determination or how — the "scheduling model" link is a pointer rather than a resolution.

- [Grounding] The note characterizes the progression "17.2x -> 4.4x tracks increasing verification strength." But the topology ordering by error amplification is 17.2x (Independent) -> 7.8x (Decentralized) -> 5.1x (Hybrid) -> 4.4x (Centralized). The note lists Centralized at 4.4x as the endpoint, but the note's own data shows Hybrid at 5.1x falls between Decentralized and Centralized. If the progression tracks "increasing verification strength," the ordering should be monotone — but Hybrid (which combines centralized and decentralized) has higher error amplification than Centralized alone. The note does not comment on why combining two verification mechanisms produces worse results than the stronger one alone, which is a potentially interesting counter-signal to the "more verification = less error" narrative. The ingest attributes Hybrid's poor showing to 515% overhead, but overhead and error amplification are different metrics.

PASS:
- [Grounding] The core contrast between Kim et al. and MAKER is well-grounded in both source ingests. The Kim et al. ingest confirms synthesis-only coordination in the Independent topology and the absence of implemented voting. The MAKER ingest confirms first-to-ahead-by-k voting, maximal decomposition, and decorrelation mechanisms. The note's central argument — that the two papers tested different aggregation operations and therefore their results are not contradictory — is a sound inference from the sources.

- [Grounding] The three MAKER design choices (maximal decomposition, hard per-step oracles, decorrelation) are accurately attributed. The MAKER ingest confirms: m=1 decomposition, deterministic per-step verification for Towers of Hanoi, and red-flagging plus temperature variation as decorrelation mechanisms. The note's claim that "None of these are present in Kim et al.'s design" is supported by the Kim et al. ingest, which confirms no decomposition, no explicit oracle, and identical prompts/tools across agents.

- [Grounding] The link to the error-correction note is accurate. That note develops the TPR > FPR condition and decorrelation requirement for voting-based error correction, and this note correctly positions voting as the error-correction mechanism and synthesis as something that does not qualify.

- [Internal consistency] The note's internal logic is consistent across sections. The distinction (synthesis propagates, voting corrects) is maintained through the Kim et al. analysis (synthesis-only = error amplification), the MAKER analysis (voting + conditions = zero errors), and the design rule (match aggregation to call relationship). No definition drift detected — "synthesis" and "voting" are used consistently throughout.

- [Internal consistency] The description field ("Synthesis propagates errors by merging all agent outputs; voting corrects errors by discarding minorities — Kim et al.'s 17.2x amplification is a synthesis failure, not evidence against multi-agent coordination") faithfully represents the body's argument without eliding the tensions acknowledged in Open Questions.

Overall: 3 warnings, 4 info

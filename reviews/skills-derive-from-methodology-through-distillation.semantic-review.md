=== SEMANTIC REVIEW: skills-derive-from-methodology-through-distillation.md ===

Claims identified: 14

1. [Evidence/"It is not codification"] Codification in this project means prompt-to-code phase transition; the medium, consumer, and verification regime change.
2. [Evidence/"It is not codification"] The methodology-to-skill relationship has none of codification's properties -- input is markdown consumed by LLM, output is markdown consumed by LLM.
3. [Evidence/"It is not constraining"] Constraining narrows the output distribution; a skill probably does produce more predictable behavior, but narrowing is a side effect, not the defining operation.
4. [Evidence/"It is not constraining"] Methodology enforcement is constraining covers enforcement reliability; this note covers derivation -- a different question.
5. [Evidence/"Distillation fits"] Five key properties: same substance/different concentration, the residue has value, the process requires judgment, the distillate can't reconstruct the source, the loss is deliberate.
6. [Evidence/"Distillation fits"] "Compilation" implies a phase transition that doesn't happen here; distillation refines the metaphor.
7. [Evidence/"The ML resonance"] ML distillation is structurally similar but is a one-shot training process, whereas KB distillation is ongoing.
8. [Reasoning/"Why the distinction matters"] Codification, constraining, and distillation describe three different operations on the same gradient (table with operation, what changes, medium transition).
9. [Reasoning/"Why the distinction matters"] Conflating them produces confused designs -- wrong mental model leads to wrong design focus.
10. [Reasoning/"Distillation is a context-budget operation"] Distillation is driven by context economics -- you can't load fifteen methodology notes every session.
11. [Reasoning/"For agents, distillation is permanent infrastructure"] For a human, distilled procedures can be transcended through understanding; for an LLM agent, distillate and source must be co-maintained indefinitely.
12. [Caveats] The chemical distillation metaphor breaks if pushed to "what are the volatile vs. non-volatile components."
13. [Caveats] Not all skills are distilled from methodology -- Cramer demonstrates distilling directly from domain artifacts without an intermediate theory-building step.
14. [Caveats] The residue is only valuable if maintained -- methodology notes that drift create a false source.

WARN:
- [Completeness/boundary] The three-term taxonomy (codification, constraining, distillation) claims these are "different operations on the same gradient," but the table shows codification has a medium transition while the other two do not. Two of the three share the "No" column for medium transition, yet the note treats all three as equidistant conceptual alternatives. A boundary case exposes the tension: a skill that is partially procedural code and partially natural language (e.g., a skill body that shells out to a Python helper to do some of its work). This sits between codification and distillation -- the note's framework has no clean place for it, and the "gradient" framing implies there should be one. The note's own codification source says "codification can also be distillation" and shows they can co-occur, which the three-row table's mutual-exclusivity framing obscures.
- [Grounding/scope mismatch] The note claims constraining is about "narrowing the output distribution" (claim 3), but the constraining.md source defines it as "narrows the space of valid interpretations an underspecified spec admits." These are different things: output distribution is a statistical property of LLM sampling; interpretation space is a semantic property of specifications. The note's characterization is a simplification that makes constraining easier to distinguish from distillation, but readers who follow the link will find a different (and broader) definition. The constraining note itself explicitly says distillation and constraining are "orthogonal" and provides a 2x2 matrix showing they can co-occur -- the reviewed note's framing of constraining as merely a "side effect" of distillation understates the source's own framework.
- [Grounding/domain coverage] The note claims the agent-statelessness note uses "compiled vs. source" as a metaphor and that "compilation implies a phase transition (source code to binary) that doesn't happen here." But the agent-statelessness note's "source vs. compiled" section is explicitly about a maintenance discipline ("methodology notes are source code; skills, routing tables, type templates, and naming conventions are compiled artifacts"), not about the metaphysics of medium transitions. The agent-statelessness note doesn't claim a phase transition is happening -- it uses "compiled" to mean "derived artifacts that must stay in sync with source." The reviewed note attributes a stronger claim to the source than the source actually makes, in order to then correct it.

INFO:
- [Completeness/boundary] The five "key properties that map well" are presented as a list without a completeness claim, but they function as a defining enumeration -- the reader takes them as "the reasons distillation is the right term." A boundary case: reversibility. Chemical distillation is reversible (you can re-combine distillate and residue). KB distillation is not straightforwardly reversible -- once reasoning is factored out and the skill exists, changing the skill doesn't regenerate the reasoning. The note's own point about "the distillate can't reconstruct the source" gestures at this, but the asymmetry is stronger than acknowledged: chemical distillation preserves all components (just separated), while KB distillation genuinely loses information (the skill doesn't contain the reasoning, and the reasoning may drift). This is closer to the "compilation" metaphor the note rejects than the "distillation" metaphor it adopts.
- [Completeness/boundary] The claim that "a different person reading the same methodology would distil a meaningfully different skill" (property 3) implies distillation is non-deterministic. But chemical distillation is highly deterministic given the same apparatus and conditions. The analogy breaks here in a way the caveats section doesn't flag -- the "requires judgment" property is actually a point of disanalogy with the chemical metaphor, not a point of alignment.
- [Internal consistency] The note says constraining is a "side effect" of distillation (Evidence section), but the Reasoning section's table lists them as independent operations on the same gradient. If constraining is a side effect of distillation, they aren't independent operations -- one follows from the other. The constraining.md source explicitly says they are "orthogonal," which is yet a third framing. The note oscillates between: (a) constraining is a side effect of distillation, (b) they are different operations on the same gradient, and (c) they are orthogonal. These three framings are not equivalent.
- [Grounding/attribution] The note says ML distillation is "a one-shot training process" (claim 7), contrasting it with KB distillation's ongoing nature. In practice, ML knowledge distillation is frequently iterative (progressive distillation, multi-stage distillation, online distillation). The contrast is overstated; the real difference is more about medium (weights vs. text) and feedback mechanism (gradient vs. judgment) than about one-shot vs. ongoing.

The source note does indeed focus on the enforcement gradient (instruction to skill to hook to script).

- [Grounding] The loading-frequency-hierarchy citation is accurate: the source note does describe the CLAUDE.md to skill-descriptions to skill-bodies to methodology hierarchy, and the reviewed note correctly identifies distillation as the process producing the skill tier.

The caveats section ("not all skills are distilled from methodology") correctly scopes the claim without contradicting it -- the title claim is about the methodology-to-skill path specifically.

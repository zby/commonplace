=== SEMANTIC REVIEW: llm-learning-phases-fall-between-human-learning-modes.md ===

Claims identified: 12

1. [Intro] "Human cognition has a hierarchy of adaptation timescales: evolution (species-level), long-term learning (years of experience), short-term learning (within a task or day), and immediate reaction (reflexive response)."
2. [Intro] "LLM training phases don't align with any one of these — they fall at intermediate positions on the spectrum."
3. [Amodei quote] Pre-training and RL exist "in the middle space between human evolution and human on-the-spot learning." In-context learning is "something between long-term human learning and short-term human learning."
4. [Why pre-training is between evolution and learning] "Humans arrive with extensive evolutionary priors — specialized brain regions, sensory integration, motor programs, social cognition circuitry."
5. [Why pre-training is between evolution and learning] "Pre-training must do double duty: it acquires both the kinds of structural priors that evolution gives humans... *and* the kinds of world knowledge that humans acquire through experience."
6. [Why in-context learning is between long-term and short-term] In-context learning "can handle surprisingly complex adaptation within a session... which is more than short-term human learning typically achieves. But it's ephemeral — nothing persists across sessions."
7. [Deploy-time learning] Deploy-time learning "resembles cultural evolution — knowledge accumulated in books, tools, and institutions rather than in individual minds — more than it resembles individual human learning."
8. [Why the non-mapping matters, point 1] "Training conflates evolution and learning." Treating pre-training as "education" misses that it also builds the basic cognitive architecture.
9. [Why the non-mapping matters, point 2] "In-context conflates learning and retrieval." Human short-term memory mostly *retrieves* from long-term stores; LLM in-context "learning" is conditioning the model's entire distribution.
10. [Why the non-mapping matters, point 3] "No LLM analogue for embodied procedural learning."
11. [Final paragraph] Rather than mapping agent memory to human memory taxonomies, "we should work from the actual properties of each LLM phase."
12. [Final paragraph] "The Amodei spectrum shows why this is necessary at the level of learning mechanisms themselves, not just at the level of document conventions."

WARN:
- [Grounding alignment] The note quotes Amodei verbatim ("We should think of pre-training — and for that matter, RL as well — as something that exists in the middle space between human evolution and human on-the-spot learning...") and attributes this to the Dwarkesh interview source. However, the captured source snapshot (`kb/sources/dario-amodei-we-are-near-the-end-of-the-exponential.md`) does not contain this quote or the spectrum framing anywhere. The source summary mentions "Continual Learning May Not Be Necessary" and lists three mechanisms (pre-training generalization, RL generalization, in-context learning) but never places them on a human-evolution-to-reaction spectrum. The ingest file (`*.ingest.md`) references the spectrum framing only by pointing back to this very note as the place that "already uses Amodei as its primary source." This creates a circular grounding chain: the note cites the source, and the ingest file cites the note, but the captured source text itself doesn't contain the quoted claim. The quote may be from the podcast audio/video that wasn't captured in the text snapshot, but as it stands the primary claim of the note cannot be verified against the linked source.

- [Completeness] The four-level human hierarchy (evolution, long-term learning, short-term learning, immediate reaction) is stated as a taxonomy of adaptation timescales, but "immediate reaction" is introduced in the opening paragraph and then never revisited. No LLM phase is mapped to or between it and short-term learning. This leaves the bottom of the spectrum unexamined. If the hierarchy is load-bearing for the argument, the note should either explain why no LLM phase falls near the reaction end or acknowledge the gap. As it stands, the four-level framing promises more coverage than the note delivers (only evolution-to-short-term is analyzed).

- [Completeness] The note claims "No LLM analogue for embodied procedural learning" (point 3 under "Why the non-mapping matters"). Boundary case: tool use and code execution. LLMs that write and execute code, use APIs, or operate robotic systems through tool-calling interfaces are acquiring something resembling procedural capability — not through physical practice, but through a feedback loop where actions produce observable results that condition subsequent behavior. The claim as stated is about embodied *physical* practice specifically (riding a bicycle, cerebellum changes), but the broader category of procedural learning (learning by doing, with feedback) has more plausible LLM analogues than the note acknowledges. The note could be read as straw-manning the procedural gap by choosing the strongest possible example (bicycle riding) rather than addressing the harder boundary cases.

INFO:

Cultural evolution is a rich concept with specific properties — it involves selection, variation, transmission fidelity, cumulative ratcheting. The note asserts the resemblance in one sentence without testing whether these properties hold for deploy-time learning. This may warrant expansion or hedging.

The note could clarify whether "suggestive" mappings have positive heuristic value or are merely not harmful.

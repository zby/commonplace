=== SEMANTIC REVIEW: human-writing-structures-transfer-to-llms-because-failure-modes-overlap.md ===

Claims identified: 14

WARN:
- [Completeness] The note enumerates three LLM failure modes that overlap with human ones: "they get distracted by irrelevant context, they conflate evidence with opinion, they skip qualifications when the argument feels strong." The Lampinen source cited does not discuss any of these three specific failure modes. Lampinen demonstrates content effects (belief bias, familiarity facilitation) on syllogisms, NLI, and Wason tasks. "Getting distracted by irrelevant context" is not among them — the source tests content that supports or conflicts with correct inferences, not irrelevant distractors. "Conflating evidence with opinion" and "skipping qualifications" are also absent from Lampinen. The enumeration appears to draw on general LLM intuitions rather than the cited evidence, but the note presents it as grounded in observed overlap. The source grounds a different (and arguably stronger) claim: LLMs show belief-bias effects that parallel human belief-bias effects.
- [Grounding — domain coverage] The note's central claim is about human writing structures (genres, conventions, Toulmin scaffold) transferring to LLMs. The Lampinen source is about content effects on logical reasoning tasks — it says nothing about writing structures, genre conventions, or document scaffolds. The inferential bridge from "LLMs show human-like content bias on reasoning tasks" to "human writing structures help LLMs for the same reasons they help humans" is the note's own argumentative move, not something the source supports. The source covers reasoning failure patterns; the note extends this to writing convention transfer. This domain gap is not acknowledged.
- [Completeness — boundary case] The note proposes the methodology: "evaluate the specific arguments for why a structure helps humans, and check whether each argument applies to LLMs." The simplest boundary case is a structure that helps humans for reasons that have no LLM analog — e.g., methods sections in scientific papers enable reproducibility (the note mentions this). But a harder boundary case sits between the enumerated items: structures that help humans via social/institutional mechanisms (peer review conventions, legal brief format enforced by courts, academic citation norms). These exist because of institutional accountability, not cognitive failure prevention. The note frames all human writing structures as evolving to prevent reasoning failures, but many evolved to satisfy institutional requirements. This category is not covered, and the per-convention evaluation methodology would need to account for it.

INFO:
- [Grounding — vocabulary mismatch] The note says "structured prompting pushes toward content-independent reasoning" and connects this to KB templates "forcing the model past content-biased defaults." Lampinen's actual finding is narrower: chain-of-thought prompting (with examples demonstrating the required reasoning type) can improve performance on abstract/unfamiliar conditions for strong models on the Wason task specifically. The note generalizes from CoT-on-Wason to "structured prompting" broadly and then to "structured templates in this KB." The inference chain (CoT helps on Wason -> structured prompting generally helps -> Toulmin sections help) involves two inferential leaps. Each is plausible but neither is grounded in the source.
- [Completeness — boundary case] The note's claim that failures like getting distracted are "un-machine-like — you wouldn't expect a computational system to get distracted" deserves scrutiny. A system trained on human-generated text to predict the next token is arguably exactly the kind of computational system you would expect to reproduce human-like content sensitivity. Lampinen makes this point explicitly: content effects may emerge from training on human-generated text. The note frames the overlap as surprising; the source frames it as a natural consequence of the training paradigm. This affects the rhetorical force of the argument but not its logical structure.
- [Internal consistency] The note says "The naive transfer argument ('it helps humans, so it helps LLMs') is weak" and then proposes per-convention evaluation as the methodology. But the concluding sentence — "syllogisms and NLI show shared failure modes (convention transfers)" — applies the methodology to reasoning tasks, not to writing conventions. No writing convention is actually evaluated using the proposed per-convention method within the note itself. The Toulmin example in paragraph 3 ("separating evidence from warrant prevents conflation — and LLMs do conflate evidence with warrant") asserts the evaluation result without performing it. The conflation claim is not grounded in any cited source.
- [Grounding — scope mismatch] The note says "LLM confidence even correlates with human response times on the same problems." Lampinen reports this correlation is statistically significant for NLI and syllogisms specifically (not all three tasks), and it is a correlation between model confidence and human RT after controlling for task variables and accuracy. The note's unqualified statement slightly overstates the scope of the finding.

PASS:
- [Grounding] The note's claim that "LLMs mirror human accuracy patterns — both perform better on familiar/believable content and worse on abstract or belief-violating content" accurately reflects Lampinen et al.'s core finding across NLI and syllogisms tasks.
- [Grounding] The Wason divergence claim — "LLMs show qualitatively different error patterns (antecedent-false errors rather than matching bias)" — is accurately attributed. Lampinen reports exactly this: LMs show fewer matching errors but more antecedent-false errors compared to humans.
- [Grounding] The note's characterization of CoT effects — "improving performance on abstract/unfamiliar conditions without degrading familiar ones" — matches Lampinen's finding that CoT pushes strong models toward content-independent reasoning primarily by lifting performance on difficult conditions.
- [Internal consistency] The note's logical structure is internally coherent: it moves from the weak naive argument, to the empirical overlap that strengthens it, to the per-convention methodology, to a concrete example of divergence (Wason). No section contradicts another.
- [Grounding] The Purdue OWL source accurately supports the note's characterization of Toulmin as separating evidence from reasoning and surfacing assumptions — the source defines grounds (evidence), warrant (connecting assumption), and backing (support for the warrant) as distinct components.

Overall: 3 warnings, 4 info

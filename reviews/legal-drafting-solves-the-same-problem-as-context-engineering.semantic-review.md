Claims identified: 15

WARN:
- [Completeness] The six-row "Techniques that transfer" table claims to show "specific techniques to narrow the interpretation space" with "direct analogues in prompt and knowledge system design." But the table omits at least two major legal drafting techniques that have plausible context engineering analogues: (1) **boilerplate / standard clauses** — pre-drafted reusable language that eliminates negotiation and interpretation variance, analogous to reusable prompt components or skill libraries; (2) **provisos and exceptions** ("notwithstanding," "except where") — explicit carve-outs from general rules, analogous to conditional overrides in routing tables or skill precedence exceptions. These are not edge cases; they are central to legal drafting practice. The table presents itself as covering "specific techniques" without a completeness qualifier, which could give readers the impression the mapping is exhaustive when it is selective.

- [Grounding alignment] The note claims "Codification is stronger constraining, not codification" and argues that statute is "still natural language, still interpreted by judges — the medium hasn't changed." But the KB's own codification note defines codification as "constraining that crosses a medium boundary" — from natural language to a symbolic medium where "the consumer changes (LLM -> interpreter) and verification becomes exact." Under this definition, statute-writing IS still constraining (not codification) because the medium remains natural language and the consumer remains a judgment-exercising interpreter. The note's heading and bold text use the word "codification" in two senses simultaneously — the KB's technical sense and the legal sense (legislatures "codify" case law into statute). The note's body correctly explains why legal codification is not KB-codification, but the heading "Codification is stronger constraining, not codification" is confusing because it relies on the reader tracking both senses without explicit disambiguation. This vocabulary collision risks readers misattributing the KB's definition to the legal domain.

- [Completeness] The note claims "legal drafting may be an equally strong source discipline" as programming, and that "Programming analogies require a translation step...Legal analogies don't — law has always worked in the underspecified medium." This overstates the directness of the analogy. Legal drafting addresses human interpreters who have decades of domain training, access to external context (legislative history, canons of construction, prior rulings), and operate under institutional constraints (stare decisis, appellate review). LLMs have none of these. The "translation step" that programming analogies supposedly require also exists for legal analogies — you must translate from "judge exercising judgment under institutional constraints with external memory" to "LLM exercising judgment under context window constraints with no persistent memory." The note treats the shared medium (natural language) as sufficient for directness while omitting that the interpreter differences are at least as significant as the medium similarities.

INFO:
- [Completeness] The mapping of KB vocabulary to legal concepts covers constraining (precedent), codification (algorithmic sentencing), distillation (legal commentary), and the constrain/relax cycle (overturning precedent). The note does not map **context engineering** itself to a legal analogue. Context engineering in the KB is "the architecture and machinery for getting the right knowledge into a bounded context at the right time." Legal practice has analogues — judicial briefing (where attorneys select and present relevant law to a judge under page limits), jury instructions (distilling applicable law for a non-expert decision-maker), and discovery (selecting which evidence enters the proceeding). The absence is not a gap in the argument, but it is a missed opportunity that would strengthen the parallel, given that the note's title specifically claims equivalence with context engineering rather than just constraining.

- [Grounding alignment] The note claims "Spec-to-program projection is judicial application" and maps the underspecified instructions framing onto judicial interpretation. The source note (agentic-systems-interpret-underspecified-instructions.md) defines spec-to-program projection as collapsing "a space of valid interpretations to one concrete program." Judicial application does collapse the interpretation space for one case, but it also generates precedent that constrains future interpretations — it is simultaneously projection AND constraining. The note acknowledges this in the later section on precedent-as-constraining, but the bold claim in "The structural parallel" section presents projection and constraining as separate when judicial application is inherently both. The two-step presentation (first projection, then constraining) may lead readers to miss that the legal domain fuses what the KB separates.

- [Internal consistency] The note says "The parallel is not metaphorical" in the opening paragraph, asserting structural identity. Yet the "Why this matters" section introduces a qualification: "This doesn't replace the programming lens — typing, testing, and compilation remain powerful." If the parallel were truly structural (not metaphorical), it would need to account for what programming provides that law does not — specifically, the formal verification and deterministic execution that law lacks. The note is not contradictory here, but the strong framing ("not metaphorical") in the opening is slightly in tension with the qualifications in the later section. The parallel is structural in one dimension (underspecified specs interpreted by judgment-exercising processors) but not in others (feedback loops, verification, deterministic compilation).

ABC's vocabulary overlap with law may simply reflect that both domains borrow from the same ordinary-language concepts (contracts, compliance, violation are everyday words). The convergence could indicate shared problem structure (as the note argues) or shared metaphorical vocabulary without shared methodology. The note presents only the first interpretation.

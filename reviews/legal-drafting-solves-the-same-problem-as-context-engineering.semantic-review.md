WARN:
- [Completeness] The note claims the parallel holds because "the spec-writing problem is the same" despite interpreter differences. But the interpreter differences are not just implementation details -- they affect the spec-writing problem itself. Legal drafters write for interpreters with stare decisis (past interpretations bind future ones), extensive external memory (legislative history, treatises, prior rulings), and institutional error correction (appellate review). Prompt engineers write for interpreters that are stateless, lack external memory, and have no institutional correction mechanism. These interpreter properties shape which spec-writing techniques work: legal canons of interpretation work partly because judges are trained to apply them; there is no analogous training guarantee for LLMs. The note acknowledges interpreter differences in the second paragraph but treats them as outside the parallel rather than as factors that change the spec-writing problem. The claim that the spec-writing problem is "the same" holds at the abstract level (narrowing interpretations of underspecified natural language) but not at the operational level (what techniques actually succeed depends on interpreter properties). This weakens the transfer claims for techniques rated "Moderate" or "Loose" in the table.

- [Grounding alignment] The note maps "Distillation is legal commentary" by claiming that "Treatises and restatements extract operational principles from masses of case law -- staying in the same medium but changing rhetorical mode from judicial reasoning to systematic exposition." The distillation note defines distillation as "compressing knowledge so that a consumer can act on it within bounded context." Legal commentary fits one aspect (compression and extraction) but diverges on a key property: distillation in the KB sense is targeted to a specific consumer-task pair under a context budget, and "different operational contexts need different extractions from the same source." Legal treatises and restatements are not targeted to a specific consumer-task pair -- they are comprehensive reference works aimed at all legal practitioners. They compress by systematizing, not by targeting. This is closer to indexing or synthesis than to distillation as the KB defines it. A tighter legal analogue to KB distillation would be a legal brief, which compresses case law for a specific judge deciding a specific case under page limits -- consumer-targeted, context-budgeted compression. The note's mapping is not wrong but uses a loose fit without flagging the looseness.

- [Completeness] The "Techniques that transfer" table assigns "Mapping tightness" ratings (Tight, Moderate, Loose) but provides no criteria for these ratings. "Defined terms" is rated Tight with explanation "same mechanism," while "Canons of interpretation" is rated Moderate because "legal canons are institutionally enforced." But institutional enforcement is not the only differentiator -- defined terms in law also have institutional enforcement (courts will apply the statutory definition section), and the mechanism is not identical (legal defined terms operate within an interpretive tradition where surrounding statutory language, legislative intent, and canons of construction constrain meaning; KB glossary entries operate without such scaffolding). The Tight/Moderate/Loose ratings appear to reflect intuitive judgment rather than a principled criterion, making them hard to verify or contest. Without criteria, a reader cannot tell whether the ratings capture a real distinction or merely the author's confidence level.

INFO:
- [Completeness] The note's title claims equivalence with "context engineering" but the body primarily maps legal concepts to constraining, codification, and distillation -- individual operations within context engineering. The note does not map legal analogues to context engineering's architectural concerns: routing (which knowledge reaches the interpreter and when), loading strategy (progressive disclosure vs. frontloading), or scoping (what gets excluded). Legal practice has clear analogues: judicial briefing (attorneys select and present relevant authorities under page limits -- a loading and scoping problem), discovery rules (what evidence is admissible -- a routing/filtering problem), and jury instructions (distilling applicable law for a non-expert decision-maker -- context engineering for a different interpreter). Mapping these would strengthen the title's claim, since currently the body supports "legal drafting solves the same problem as constraining" more than "the same problem as context engineering."

- [Internal consistency] The second paragraph states: "The interpreters differ significantly, but the spec-writing problem is the same." The "Why this matters" section states: "legal analogies require translating from one kind of interpreter (institutionally constrained judges with external memory) to another (stateless LLMs with bounded context). Neither transfer is free." The first statement presents interpreter differences as irrelevant to the core claim; the second acknowledges they impose a translation cost. These are reconcilable (the abstract problem is the same; concrete translations are needed) but the note does not explicitly reconcile them. A reader could take the opening as claiming the analogy is frictionless and the closing as walking it back.

- [Grounding alignment] The ABC section claims "The convergence could reflect shared problem structure (as this note argues) or simply shared metaphorical vocabulary; the paper doesn't cite law as a source discipline, so the connection is circumstantial." This is an admirably honest hedge. However, the note then uses ABC's hard/soft constraint hierarchy to partially answer the open question about interpretation hierarchies, treating it as evidence. The hedge and the use are in mild tension: if the convergence might be mere vocabulary overlap, the hard/soft hierarchy might not genuinely map to constitutional/statutory/regulatory hierarchy -- the structural similarity could be superficial. The ABC ingest note confirms the paper routes through Design-by-Contract (Meyer, 1988), not law, which supports treating the convergence as independent discovery but does not resolve whether the discovered structures are genuinely homologous.

- [Completeness] The civil law / common law distinction is introduced in the structural parallel section as evidence that "projection and constraining are genuinely separable." This is a valuable observation, but the note does not follow through on the prompt engineering implication. If common law fuses projection and constraining (each ruling both resolves a case and creates precedent) while civil law keeps them apart (judges apply codes without generating binding precedent), what does the civil law model look like in prompt engineering? A system where each LLM call resolves its task but generates no persistent constraining artifact? That would be the default ephemeral-session model. The open question about common law vs. civil law mapping to prompt engineering styles is noted but the note already contains enough material to sketch an answer.

PASS:
- [Internal consistency] The mapping between KB vocabulary and legal concepts is internally consistent across all sections. Constraining maps to precedent (narrowing interpretation space through repeated consistent rulings), codification maps to algorithmic sentencing and smart contracts (crossing the medium boundary from natural language to executable code), distillation maps to legal commentary (extraction from a larger body), and the constrain/relax cycle maps to overturning precedent. Each mapping respects the definitions in the source notes (constraining.md, codification.md, distillation.md). No pairwise contradictions found.

- [Grounding alignment] The note's use of the underspecified-instructions framing is accurate. The source note (agentic-systems-interpret-underspecified-instructions.md) defines semantic underspecification as a property of the specification language, not the execution engine, and spec-to-program projection as collapsing "a space of valid interpretations to one concrete program." The legal-drafting note correctly applies both concepts: statutes are underspecified natural language specs, and judicial application collapses the space for a specific case.

- [Internal consistency] The note's codification analysis is self-consistent. The heading "Statute codification is stronger constraining, not KB-codification" uses "codification" in two clearly different senses (legal and KB), and the body explicitly disambiguates: "In the KB's vocabulary, codification requires crossing a medium boundary... Statute-writing doesn't cross that boundary." The note correctly identifies that statute is more authoritative than case law (stronger constraining) without claiming it changes the medium. This is consistent with the codification note's definition.

- [Grounding alignment] The note correctly characterizes the ABC paper's relationship to its thesis. The ABC ingest confirms: ABC extends Design-by-Contract to agents, uses contracts/compliance/violation/recovery vocabulary, does not cite law as a source discipline, and introduces hard/soft constraint hierarchy. The note's claim that ABC "provides independent support" is appropriately qualified as "circumstantial" and the alternative explanation (shared metaphorical vocabulary) is noted.

- [Completeness] The note's self-identified gaps (boilerplate and provisos/exceptions absent from the table, open questions about specific legal techniques and common/civil law mapping) demonstrate awareness of the enumeration's boundaries. The note does not claim exhaustiveness for the technique table and explicitly flags what is missing. This is good practice for a seedling-status note.

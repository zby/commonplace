## Summary

The paper traces the ancestry of W. Edwards Deming's Plan-Do-Study-Act (PDSA) cycle from Galileo and Bacon through American pragmatism (Peirce, James, Dewey, C.I. Lewis) to Shewhart's 1939 specification-production-inspection circle, Deming's 1950 "Deming Wheel," the Japanese recasting into Plan-Do-Check-Act (PDCA) around 1951, and Deming's own PDSA formulations of 1986 and 1993. Its through-line argument is that PDSA formalizes the scientific method as an iterative learn-and-improve loop, and that PDCA and PDSA are related only through that shared method — Deming himself, in cited letters and transcripts, rejected PDCA as a "corruption" and disowned its authorship. The load-bearing addition for this KB is the 1991 Moen/Nolan/Provost move: the Plan step must state a prediction and its associated theory, and the Study step compares observed data against that prediction, supplying "the deductive/inductive interplay necessary for learning."

## Extractable Value

1. **Independent-tradition corroboration of the discovery-lifecycle core.** A Deming-lineage improvement cycle deliberately builds prediction+theory into Plan and observation-versus-prediction into Study — the same abduction/deduction/induction interplay the lifecycle note adapts from Peirce. This is the highest-reach item: convergence of two unrelated traditions is evidence the learning core is real and general, not an artifact of one philosophy. [experiment]
2. **Reach-over-adaptive-fit, restated from improvement science.** "It is not enough to determine that a change resulted in improvement during a particular test... you will need to be able to predict whether a change will result in improvement under the different conditions you will face in the future" is near-verbatim support for the first-principles note's explanatory-reach claim. 3. **The Check→Study shift as a gate-vs-critique datapoint.** Deming rejected "check" because it "means to hold back" and renamed the step Study — compare observed data to prediction as a basis for learning. This distinguishes a pass/fail compliance check (PDCA, "implementation and compliance") from a learning step (PDSA, "testing and learning"), which maps onto the review system's verdict/gate versus report/critique distinction and is reusable framing for why a learning step is not a pass/fail gate. ## Limitations (our opinion)

This is editorial opinion. Read as participant-historian advocacy, not neutral history. The author is a Deming insider with a stake in the thesis: the paper is structured to establish PDSA as the correct lineage and PDCA as a "corruption," and much of its strongest evidence is Deming's own interested testimony (personal letters to the author, a GAO transcript, archival remarks). The central claim that PDSA "formalizes the scientific method" and supplies "the deductive/inductive interplay" is asserted by mapping cycle steps onto abduction/deduction/induction — an analogy, never tested, and vulnerable to the "naming something is not explaining it" failure. The account of the Japanese PDCA's origins is secondhand and admittedly unresolved ("no one person claims authorship... I found no evidence to dispute Imai's translation"). None of this undercuts the corroboration value above — an independent tradition converging on the same loop is useful precisely because the author was not reading our KB's Peircean framing — but the paper should not be cited as an empirical test of the learning core, only as documentary evidence that the core recurs.

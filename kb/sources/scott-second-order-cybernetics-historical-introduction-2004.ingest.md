## Summary

Scott reconstructs second-order cybernetics as a development internal to early cybernetics rather than a disconnected invention. He reports von Foerster's canonical distinction: first-order cybernetics studies observed systems, second-order cybernetics studies observing systems (journal p. 1373; PDF p. 10). Natural-science hypothesis testing remains possible at first order, but those systems are defined from second-order perspectives and understandings. The paper situates this move among circular causality, constructivist epistemology, autopoiesis, conversation theory, and reflexive metadisciplinary work without collapsing those programs into one definition.

## Inherited Vocabulary

### Exact terminology and definitions

- **First-order cybernetics:** study of “observed systems”; **second-order cybernetics:** study of “observing systems” (journal p. 1373; PDF p. 10).
- **Cybernetics of cybernetics:** a fully reflexive metadisciplinary activity in which cybernetics studies its own workings (journal p. 1367; PDF p. 4).
- **Observer epistemology:** first-order systems and explanations are defined from the perspectives of observing systems rather than from a view nowhere (journal p. 1373; PDF p. 10).

### Necessary conditions versus illustrative features

The observed/observing distinction is the defining historical core. Autopoiesis, conversation theory, radical constructivism, self-observing objects, and sociocybernetics are developments or neighboring programs, not necessary features of every second-order analysis.

### Assumed system boundary and people as components

The second-order boundary includes the observer whose perspective defines the first-order system. Scott's history repeatedly treats scientists and social participants as observing systems; people can therefore be components of the broader observing system, although the article does not supply a formal membership test.

### Causal structure

Observation and explanation are circular: observers construct domains and descriptions that organize subsequent action and inquiry, while participation in those domains changes the observers' understandings. The paper is historical and epistemological, not a formal causal model.

### Constraints for later Commonplace notes

Use Scott to place terms historically and to keep neighboring traditions distinct. Use von Foerster for the primary definition. Do not infer causal self-representation, introspection, or intercession from “observing system” alone, and do not decide Commonplace's classification here.

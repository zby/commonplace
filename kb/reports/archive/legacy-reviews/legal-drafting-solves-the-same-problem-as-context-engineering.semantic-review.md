=== SEMANTIC REVIEW: legal-drafting-solves-the-same-problem-as-context-engineering.md ===

Claims identified: 20

1. "Context engineering for LLMs is writing natural language instructions interpreted by a processor exercising judgment. Law has been doing this for centuries." (opening)
2. "The parallel is structural rather than merely metaphorical" (opening)
3. "both domains face semantic underspecification as a property of natural language" (opening)
4. "The interpreters differ enough that techniques do not transfer intact at the operational level, but the abstract spec-writing problem is the same." (opening, paragraph 2)
5. "A statute is a natural language specification that a judge must interpret, resolving its ambiguity through judgment rather than compilation." (The structural parallel)
6. "Judicial application resolves the spec into a case outcome." (The structural parallel)
7. "In common law systems, judicial application is also constraining: each ruling generates precedent that narrows future interpretation, fusing what the KB vocabulary separates (resolution and constraining)." (The structural parallel)
8. "This split is itself informative: it shows that resolution and constraining are genuinely separable, not inevitably fused." (The structural parallel, on civil vs common law)
9. "No natural language specification eliminates all ambiguity." (The structural parallel)
10. "Two judges can reach different conclusions from the same statute and the same facts — just as two LLM runs can produce different outputs from the same prompt." (The structural parallel)

WARN:
- [completeness] The techniques table enumerates 6 legal techniques and flags 2 absent ones (boilerplate, provisos), but omits **legislative history / travaux preparatoires** -- the practice of using drafting records to resolve ambiguity. This has a direct context engineering analogue (commit history, design rationale in ADRs, methodology notes that explain why a convention exists). It is a distinct mechanism from any of the six listed: it narrows interpretation by appeal to authorial intent rather than by constraining the text itself. The note's own second paragraph mentions judges' "access to external context (legislative history, prior rulings)" as a difference between the interpreters, yet this same mechanism appears in the spec-writing toolbox and is absent from the techniques table.
- [completeness] The techniques table also omits **severability / modular interpretation** -- the legal principle that if one clause is invalid or inapplicable, the rest of the document still stands. This maps to modular prompt/instruction design (one skill failing does not invalidate the routing table). Given the note claims to survey "specific techniques to narrow the interpretation space," a technique about managing partial failure within a specification is a notable gap.
- [grounding-alignment] The note claims "The constrain/relax cycle is overturning precedent" and draws the parallel: "The same cycle operates in prompt engineering -- a constrained convention encounters new requirements and gets relaxed back to open-ended guidance." The constraining note defines relaxing as "replacing a constrained component with a general-purpose one" -- returning to an underspecified state. But overturning precedent is not a return to openness; courts replace one specific interpretation with a different specific interpretation. There is rarely a genuine period of underspecification between the old ruling and the new one. The legal analogue maps more tightly to *re-constraining* (constrain differently) than to *relaxing* (return to underspecified). The note's own wording ("relax, then a new line of cases constrains a different reading") implies a two-step process, but in practice the "relax" phase is vestigial in most overturning scenarios.
- [grounding-alignment] Claim 20: the note says ABC's hard/soft constraint hierarchy "answers" the open question about interpretation hierarchies (constitution > statute > regulation > case law). But these are different organizing principles. The legal hierarchy is about **source authority** -- which source prevails when sources conflict. ABC's hard/soft distinction is about **violation tolerance** -- zero-tolerance invariants vs. transient violations with bounded recovery. A constitutional provision does not merely have lower tolerance for violation than a regulation; it has higher authority. An ABC hard constraint does not outrank a soft constraint by authority -- it simply permits no violations at all. The mapping is suggestive but "answers" overstates the fit; "partially addresses" or "is suggestive for" would be more accurate. The ABC ingest confirms the paper routes through Design-by-Contract (Meyer, 1988), not through legal authority hierarchies.

INFO:
- [completeness] The "mapping tightness" dimension (tight/moderate/loose) is a useful analytical addition, but the criteria are defined only in a single prose sentence: "how much of the mechanism carries over without depending on interpreter-specific institutions." Boundary cases between categories are ambiguous. For example, "Enumeration" is rated tight, but legal enumeration carries implicit closure rules (expressio unius est exclusio alterius -- naming some items excludes unlisted ones) that have no automatic analogue in prompt schemas, where the LLM may treat an enum as suggestive rather than exhaustive. The tightness rating may be slightly overstated for this row.
- [completeness] The note's title and thesis claim the parallel is with "context engineering," but the body primarily maps legal concepts to constraining, codification, and distillation -- individual operations within context engineering. The note does not map legal analogues to context engineering's architectural concerns: routing (which knowledge reaches the interpreter and when), loading strategy (progressive disclosure vs. frontloading), or scoping (what gets excluded). Legal practice has clear analogues for these: judicial briefing (attorneys select and present relevant authorities under page limits -- a loading and scoping problem), discovery rules (what evidence is admissible -- a routing/filtering problem), and jury instructions (distilling applicable law for a non-expert decision-maker). As it stands, the body supports "legal drafting solves the same problem as constraining" more strongly than "the same problem as context engineering."
- [internal-consistency] Claim 10: "Two judges can reach different conclusions from the same statute and the same facts -- just as two LLM runs can produce different outputs from the same prompt." The underspecified instructions note carefully distinguishes semantic underspecification (multiple valid interpretations) from execution indeterminism (different outputs across runs due to sampling). Two judges disagreeing is closer to underspecification (different valid interpretations of the same text). "Two LLM runs" producing different outputs could be either phenomenon. The parallel is not wrong, but it conflates what the foundation note separates. If the point is about underspecification (the deeper parallel), a more precise phrasing would compare two judges to two LLMs (or two prompt designs) rather than two runs of the same LLM.
- [grounding-alignment] The note's opening says "LLMs have none of these" (referring to "decades of domain training, access to external context, institutional constraints"). This slightly overstates the asymmetry. LLMs have extensive training data (an analogue to domain training, though different in kind), can access external context via tool use and retrieval (an analogue to legislative history, though not institutionally curated), and operate under deployment-time constraints (system prompts, RLHF alignment, guardrails -- an analogue to institutional constraints, though without appellate review). The qualifier "At the abstract level" in the next sentence partially mitigates this, but the sentence as written could mislead a reader who stops there.

PASS:
- [completeness] The claim that "the parallel is structural rather than merely metaphorical" is well-supported. The note identifies a shared formal situation (natural-language spec + judgment-exercising interpreter + irreducible ambiguity) and grounds it in the underspecified instructions framing. Boundary case tested: a purely mechanical legal process (e.g., calculating a filing deadline from a statute's explicit days count) -- this maps to the deterministic code side of the boundary, not to the legal-drafting-as-context-engineering parallel, confirming the note is correctly scoped to the judgment-requiring cases.
- [completeness] The common law / civil law split as evidence that "resolution and constraining are genuinely separable" (claim 8) is a well-constructed argument. The note uses the existence of two different legal systems to demonstrate that spec resolution and constraining need not be fused. This is a legitimate analytical move -- the civil law system resolves specs (judges apply codes to cases) without generating binding precedent (no constraining feedback loop).
- [grounding-alignment] The distinction between "statute codification" (stronger constraining, still natural language) and KB-codification (crossing the medium boundary to code) is precise and well-grounded in the codification note's definition: "codification requires crossing a medium boundary (natural language to executable code, changing the consumer from a judgment-exercising interpreter to a deterministic runtime)." The note correctly identifies that statute-writing narrows interpretation without changing the medium.
- [grounding-alignment] The distillation mapping is appropriately caveated. The note says "only loosely in commentary" and identifies legal briefs as "the tighter analogue: it compresses authorities for a specific decision-maker and case under explicit page and attention limits." The distillation note defines distillation as "compressing knowledge so that a consumer can act on it within bounded context" with emphasis on consumer-task pairing and context budget. Legal briefs match this definition (specific judge, specific case, page limits). Legal commentary/treatises are a looser fit (comprehensive reference work, not consumer-task targeted). The note's two-tier mapping (commentary = loose, briefs = tight) accurately reflects the distillation note's definition.
- [grounding-alignment] The note's use of the underspecified instructions framing is accurate throughout. The source note defines semantic underspecification as "a property of the specification language itself, not the engine" and spec-to-program projection as collapsing "a space of valid interpretations to one concrete program." The legal-drafting note applies both concepts faithfully: statutes are underspecified natural language specs, and judicial application collapses the space for a specific case.
- [grounding-alignment] The note correctly characterizes ABC's relationship to its thesis. The ABC ingest confirms: ABC extends Design-by-Contract to agents, uses contracts/compliance/violation/recovery vocabulary, does not cite law as a source discipline, and introduces hard/soft constraint hierarchy. The note's claim that ABC "provides independent support" is appropriately qualified as "circumstantial" -- an honest hedge that acknowledges the alternative explanation (shared metaphorical vocabulary rather than shared problem structure).
- [internal-consistency] The note maintains a consistent position on transfer limits: "techniques do not transfer intact at the operational level" (opening) is operationalized in the techniques table (via the "mapping tightness" column with explicit tight/moderate/loose ratings) and recapitulated in the conclusion ("Neither transfer is free, but they address different gaps"). No section contradicts this framing.
- [internal-consistency] The note's treatment of constraining vs. codification is internally consistent. Section 3 argues statute-writing is "stronger constraining, not KB-codification" because the medium does not change, and the techniques table classifies "statute codification" at moderate tightness (both commit an interpretation, but statutes carry legal authority). These two treatments align.

Overall: 4 warnings, 4 info

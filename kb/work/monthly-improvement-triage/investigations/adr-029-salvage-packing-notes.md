# Investigation: ADR 029 salvage and packing-axis insights — do either warrant a standalone note?

## Source item

From `kb/work/connect-maintenance-observations/README.md`: "Review-bundling proposal lacked separate rationale notes for salvage and packing-axis insights. ADR 029 now accepted the pair-protocol design and states salvage and packing-choice consequences. No separate transferable notes were written. Decide whether ADR 029 is enough or whether the salvage/packing insights need standalone notes."

## What ADR 029 actually says

`kb/reference/adr/029-review-execution-unified-on-note-gate-pairs.md` (accepted 2026-06-12) unifies review execution on one `(note, gate)` pair grammar (`=== PAIR REVIEW START: {note_path} :: {gate_id} ===`), one renderer, one parser, and one finalization path, replacing two divergent execution paths that previously differed only in which axis (note or gate) they held constant when packing pairs into a call.

**(a) Packing axis.** Decision point 5: "Batching is a packing choice, not a protocol. Note-packed jobs and gate-packed jobs both use the same pair grammar, parser, and finalization path. The parent agent or harness owns fan-out; Commonplace owns deterministic job creation and finalization." Point 6 adds that per-job artifacts are "pair-grammar slices" — naming and manifest structure derive from the job's packing strategy, not from a second protocol. Consequence: "Mixed-axis packing (arbitrary pairs per call) is structurally supported by the renderer/parser boundary, available if a future caller needs it." The rationale is squarely about *why the grammar is keyed the way it is*: keying every response block by the full `note_path :: gate_id` composite, not by position or by a single held-constant axis, is what lets note-packed and gate-packed jobs — and any future mixed-axis packing — share one grammar/renderer/parser instead of needing per-axis code.

**(b) Salvage.** ADR 029's own text only touches this in one line of its Consequences: "Pair coverage failures are represented uniformly; ADR 035 later made live finalization all-or-nothing instead of salvaging completed reviews from failed jobs." The real reasoning lives in `kb/reference/adr/035-review-jobs-finalize-all-or-nothing-with-derived-artifacts.md` (accepted 2026-07-01), which superseded-in-part ADR 029. ADR 035's Context: "`review_pairs` stored `pair_status`, so failed multi-pair jobs could retain completed pairs. ... Partial salvage made acceptance reasoning harder because a failed job could still advance freshness for a subset of pairs." Its Decision: "Finalization is all-or-nothing... Missing, duplicate, unexpected, malformed, or result-less pair blocks fail the whole job. Failed jobs leave pair decisions null and write no acceptance rows." Its accepted cost: "A mostly complete multi-pair output with one missing pair must be rerun or repaired outside finalization; the completed subset is not accepted." So the salvage insight's actual shape is: *partial completion of a batch was rejected specifically because letting a subset succeed would let freshness/acceptance state silently advance on a job that never fully finished* — a tradeoff of wasted already-done work against a simpler, safer completeness invariant.

## Reach test (`kb/notes/COLLECTION.md`)

### Packing axis

- **Change one premise, predict the conclusion?** Yes. Premise: grammar keys a response block by full composite identity vs. by position/single-axis. Change it (key by position only) and the conclusion changes predictably: adding a second axis now requires a second renderer/parser/failure-policy, and mixed-axis batches become inexpressible — exactly the "twin parsers/rewriters" defect ADR 029's own Context section names as the problem it fixed.
- **Would it apply in a different domain?** Yes, cleanly, to any batched LLM-call protocol with more than one plausible grouping axis (e.g., batched extraction over a (document, field) grid, batched grading over a (student, question) grid). The mechanism is general: probabilistic reconstruction of a keyed structure from free text needs the key inside the grammar, because there is no independent index to recover identity from if the model reorders, drops, or duplicates a block — unlike ordinary exact-parse batch APIs (JSON-RPC batch ids, GraphQL aliases) where a positional convention often survives.
- **Could someone say exactly how it's wrong, not just incomplete?** Yes — a real, locatable objection exists: the decoupling can break if the *requested content* per block legitimately needs to differ by axis (e.g., a cross-item comparison field that only makes sense for one grouping choice). That is a genuine boundary, not just "more work needed," so the claim is falsifiable in the way `kb/notes/COLLECTION.md` asks for.

**Verdict: WRITE-NOTE.** This clearly outruns ADR 029: the ADR records a system-specific decision (unify review execution on one pair grammar); the general mechanism — full-identity keys decouple a batched LLM-call protocol from its packing/grouping policy, and this matters more for LLM output than for exact-parse APIs because parsing is probabilistic reconstruction, not deterministic decode — applies to any batched LLM-call system with more than one plausible grouping axis, independent of review-bundling.

### Salvage

- **Change one premise, predict the conclusion?** Weakly. The stated mechanism — "salvage lets freshness advance on an incomplete unit of work, which is unsafe when downstream logic assumes completion means full coverage" — does predict its own conclusion, but the premise itself ("downstream logic assumes full coverage") is doing most of the work, and that premise is local to this system's freshness/acceptance design, not a property that transfers by default.
- **Would it apply in a different domain?** Only under heavy qualification. Whole-job-failure-over-partial-salvage is standard transactional/atomicity wisdom (all-or-nothing commits protecting invariants) that is not novel to this KB's context-engineering theory — it is general software-engineering knowledge that predates and sits outside this project's contribution. The KB-specific angle (why *this* review system chose whole-failure) reduces almost entirely to the same asymmetric-cost argument `a-derived-copy-of-recomputable-truth-must-be-checked-or-absent.md` already makes for derived copies: bounded, recoverable cost (redo) beats a silent, unbounded one (a completion signal that consumers trust but that doesn't mean what they think it means).
- **Could someone say exactly how it's wrong, not just incomplete?** Yes, easily, and that is itself the problem: plenty of real batch/checkpointing systems correctly choose partial-credit salvage (e.g., a crawler that persists completed pages and resumes only the failed subset) precisely because their downstream consumer *does* track per-item coverage. So a general claim "salvage is unsafe" would be false on its face; the true, narrow claim ("salvage is unsafe *specifically when* the completion signal feeds a downstream invariant that assumes full coverage and doesn't itself track the subset") is a conditional that is really just an instance of the checked-or-absent asymmetry, not a new mechanism.

**Verdict: DISMISS.** The transferable content the salvage insight carries is not new to this KB — it is the same silent-unbounded-vs-bounded-recoverable-cost asymmetry already stated generally in `a-derived-copy-of-recomputable-truth-must-be-checked-or-absent.md`, applied here to a job's completion status rather than a cached derived fact. Writing a standalone note would either restate that asymmetry under new domain vocabulary (violating the theory-independence expectation that a note's claim survive with any single cited description removed) or overclaim a general "salvage is bad" rule that real partial-credit systems elsewhere falsify. ADR 035's Consequences section is the right and sufficient home for the system-specific rationale.

**Not acted on, flagged for a future pass:** whether the salvage case is concrete enough to add as a fifth instance to `a-derived-copy-of-recomputable-truth-must-be-checked-or-absent.md`'s "Instances across four surfaces" section is a real open question, but it is an edit to an existing, carefully curated note rather than a new standalone note, and is out of scope for this triage's WRITE-NOTE/DISMISS decision.

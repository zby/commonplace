# Investigation: four-source OOD-failure convergence (SuperARC, EsoLang-Bench, Pathway/Sudoku-Bench, Ebrahimi induction-bias)

## Claim under test

From `kb/log.md` (SYNTHESIS entry, also carried in the current triage README's "Big-potential / high-effort" section):

> Four sources now converge on "LLMs score near zero on well-specified problems that strip training-distribution shortcuts": SuperARC (recursive compression), EsoLang-Bench (code generation OOD), Pathway/Sudoku-Bench (constraint satisfaction), Ebrahimi induction-bias (state tracking). SuperARC adds a fourth independent domain with the strongest methodology (AIT-grounded, mathematically proven equivalence between compression and prediction).

Also flagged `PROMOTE` in `kb/work/log-triage-2026-04-27.md` (now folded into the current triage), unwritten for over two months.

## What I read

All four full sources (both `.md` snapshot and `.ingest.md` where both exist), in full:

- `kb/sources/superarc-ait-benchmark-llm-compression-abstraction.md` + `.ingest.md` — AIT-grounded benchmark. Frontier LLMs score φ = 0.007–0.042 (near zero) on binary-sequence recursive compression; a purpose-built neuro-symbolic AIXI/BDM/CTM baseline scores 1.000. Most "correct" LLM programs are bare print statements — zero compression. Critically, **LLM performance improves dramatically on integer sequences** (attributed to memorization of common mathematical sequences), which is the paper's own explanation for why binary sequences are needed for unbiased evaluation. This is a direct, explicit training-distribution-shortcut-removal design: hold "recursive compression" fixed, remove the memorization shortcut (binary vs. integer), watch the score collapse to near zero.
- `kb/sources/esolang-bench-evaluating-genuine-reasoning-via-esoteric-programming.md` + `.ingest.md` — verified directly in the raw source (not just the ingest): "models achieving 85–95% on standard benchmarks score only 0–11% on equivalent esoteric tasks, with 0% accuracy beyond the Easy tier" (lines 28–31, confirmed again in the results tables at lines 540–568: all models 0% on Medium/Hard/Extra-Hard across all five esoteric languages). The design explicitly holds the *algorithmic problem* fixed (e.g., factorial, prime counting — the same 80 problems used for the standard-benchmark comparison) and moves it into syntax with minimal training-data incentive. This is the same shortcut-removal design as SuperARC, targeting a different shortcut (familiar syntax/tokens instead of memorized sequences).
- `kb/sources/pathway-beyond-transformers-sudoku-bench.md` + `.ingest.md` — a Pathway (vendor) blog post, not a paper. BDH (Pathway's own undisclosed model) scores 97.4% on ~250k extreme Sudoku puzzles; O3-mini/DeepSeek R1/Claude 3.7 score 0%. The blog's own explanation for the 0% is **architectural**: transformers process token-by-token with a bounded per-token internal state (~1,000 floats) and "cannot hold multiple candidate strategies in parallel" — not "the training-distribution shortcut was removed." There is no claim in this source that Sudoku strips a memorization shortcut the way binary sequences or esoteric syntax do; LLMs plausibly have plenty of Sudoku-strategy text in training. The ingest independently flags severe evidence-quality problems: BDH's architecture is completely undisclosed, the LLM evaluation methodology is unstated (which model versions, what prompting, was tool use allowed), the vendor has a direct commercial incentive, and the ingest explicitly ranks this source weakest of any in the cluster ("Ebrahimi >> ConvexBench > Pathway").
- `kb/sources/induction-bias-sequence-models-ebrahimi-2026.md` + `.ingest.md` — 190k-run empirical study (Qualcomm AI Research) on modular addition and permutation composition. Read the full paper, not just the ingest. The abstract itself states the paper's contribution is a **reframing away from OOD**: "a growing body of literature has shown this limitation primarily through failures in out-of-distribution (OOD) generalization... In this work, we shift attention to the in-distribution implications." The paper's central, quantified result is a **data-efficiency and weight-sharing** finding (the sharing factor κ: transformers show κ ≈ 1 or κ < 1 — length-isolated learning, even destructive interference at κ = 0.28 for CoT — while RNNs show κ >> 1). Transformers are not shown scoring "near zero" in-distribution; with enough data (or the right supervision format, e.g. CoT), they do learn the tasks — the finding is that they need vastly more data and don't amortize across lengths. The one place a literal "near-zero" score does appear is Appendix B.1 (Table 2): transformers get near-chance accuracy on OOD length-generalization (2× training length) while RNNs get ~1.00. That IS a same-shape shortcut-removal result (train on short sequences, strip the "seen this exact length" shortcut, extrapolate) — but it is a secondary/appendix result, not the paper's headline claim, and the paper explicitly distinguishes it from the in-distribution data-inefficiency story that is its main contribution.

## Reach test (`kb/notes/COLLECTION.md`)

- **Change one premise, predict the conclusion change?** For SuperARC + EsoLang-Bench: yes, cleanly. Both are literally designed as "hold the algorithmic problem fixed, vary how much training-distribution familiarity the surface form carries, predict the score collapses when familiarity is removed" — and both report exactly that. This is a real, hard-to-vary mechanism shared by two sources.
  For Pathway/Sudoku and Ebrahimi, this does not hold across all four uniformly. Pathway's own account of *why* LLMs score 0% never invokes shortcut-removal at all — it invokes a fixed architectural bottleneck (bounded per-token state), a claim that would predict 0% even on a *familiar*, heavily-trained-on constraint problem, which is a different premise than "no shortcut available." Ebrahimi's headline claim (data efficiency, weight sharing) is a story about *how much data is needed*, not about scores collapsing to zero once distributional shortcuts are stripped — the near-zero-score evidence in that paper is a secondary appendix result (OOD length extrapolation), not the mechanism the paper argues for.
- **Would it apply in a different domain?** The narrower, well-evidenced version — "benchmarks that hold a problem fixed while removing the LLM's ability to pattern-match against memorized/familiar training distribution reveal near-zero underlying capability" — transfers cleanly and is already the exact claim `first-principles-reasoning-selects-for-explanatory-reach-over.md` makes (adaptive fit vs. explanatory reach), with SuperARC as its worked example. EsoLang-Bench is a second, independent worked example of the *same* claim, not a new claim.
  The broader four-source version, stitched together, is actually a claim about *two or three different mechanisms* wearing one label: (1) shortcut-stripping causes collapse [SuperARC, EsoLang-Bench — strong], (2) lack of architectural induction bias causes data inefficiency and, secondarily, OOD-length collapse [Ebrahimi — real but a distinct mechanism, already well-homed in the bitter-lesson-boundary cluster], (3) bounded per-token state causes constraint-satisfaction failure [Pathway — asserted, not demonstrated, and the weakest-quality source in the set].
- **Could someone say exactly how it's wrong, not just incomplete?** Yes: the four sources do not test the same independent variable. SuperARC and EsoLang-Bench manipulate *distributional familiarity of the surface form* while holding the task's difficulty class fixed. Ebrahimi manipulates *sequence length and supervision format* and is explicitly an in-distribution-efficiency story, with the OOD near-zero result being incidental (Table 2, not the paper's argued mechanism). Pathway doesn't manipulate familiarity at all — it argues an architectural capacity bound that would apply even to problems abundant in training data, and its methodology is undisclosed enough that the 0% claim is close to unfalsifiable as currently reported. Calling all four "the same convergence" would misattribute Ebrahimi's and Pathway's findings to a mechanism their own authors don't argue for.

## Verdict: DISMISS (four-source framing); genuine two-source convergence already has a home

The four-source convergence as stated in `kb/log.md` overstates its own uniformity. There is a real, tight, two-source convergence — SuperARC and EsoLang-Bench independently designed benchmarks around the identical mechanism (hold the problem fixed, strip training-distribution familiarity from the surface form, watch scores collapse to near zero) and both explicitly frame their own contribution this way. But that claim is not new: it is exactly the claim already made by `kb/notes/first-principles-reasoning-selects-for-explanatory-reach-over.md`, which already uses SuperARC as its worked example. EsoLang-Bench is additional evidence for an *existing* note's *existing* claim, not the seed of a new one — writing a new "four-source convergence" note would either (a) duplicate that note's thesis under a new title, violating body-composability by fragmenting one claim across two homes, or (b) inflate the claim's scope by folding in Ebrahimi and Pathway, whose own papers argue different mechanisms (data-efficiency/weight-sharing, and undisclosed-architecture capacity bound, respectively) that don't reduce to "strips training-distribution shortcuts."

Pathway/Sudoku-Bench in particular should not anchor a KB claim at all in its current form: undisclosed model architecture, undisclosed LLM-eval methodology, direct vendor commercial incentive, and the ingest's own assessment ranks it the weakest source in any comparison set it appears in. Its inclusion would drag down the credibility of a note whose other three legs (SuperARC, EsoLang-Bench, Ebrahimi) are all methodologically serious.

Ebrahimi is well-evidenced but is already correctly homed as *architectural induction-bias / data-efficiency* evidence (in `codification-and-relaxing-navigate-the-bitter-lesson-boundary.md` and `agent-context-is-constrained-by-soft-degradation-not-hard-token-limits.md`), a sibling claim to shortcut-stripping, not an instance of it.

**Recommended action (not performed here, out of this investigation's scope):** add EsoLang-Bench to `kb/notes/first-principles-reasoning-selects-for-explanatory-reach-over.md` as a second worked example alongside SuperARC's integer-vs-binary gap — this is genuinely low-hanging fruit (the EsoLang-Bench ingest's own "Recommended Next Action" already asked for exactly this, and it never landed). That single edit fully captures the strong part of the convergence claim without inventing a new note or overstating what Ebrahimi and Pathway actually show. Pathway/Sudoku-Bench does not currently warrant a link into `kb/notes/` given its evidence quality; it can stay source-only unless independently corroborated (e.g., if the underlying arXiv 2506.21734 LLM-accuracy citation or BDH's architecture becomes disclosed and checkable).

**Reopening condition:** if a fifth or later source runs the *same shortcut-stripping design* (hold problem fixed, strip a specific training-distribution shortcut, report near-zero collapse) in a domain SuperARC/EsoLang-Bench don't cover, the two-source pattern would become a three-source pattern worth a dedicated note of its own — at that point it would be citing a real, repeated, single-mechanism finding rather than a label stretched over heterogeneous designs.

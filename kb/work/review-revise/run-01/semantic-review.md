WARN:
- [completeness] The three-type trace taxonomy ("conversation transcripts," "tool/action traces," "reasoning traces") is introduced as "at least three trace types" — a hedge that acknowledges incompleteness. But the rest of the note treats these three as the operative partition. A notable missing type is **structured intermediate artifacts** — partial syntheses, relevance labels, extracted claim lists, sub-goal decompositions. These are explicitly listed in the bounded-context orchestration model as outputs of bounded calls (line 43-44 of that note: "relevance label, claim list, cluster summary, contradiction table, partial synthesis, sub-goal set, or satisfaction signal"). They are neither conversation transcripts nor tool traces nor reasoning traces — they are judgment outputs that accumulate in K. They also have a different loading profile: they are often exactly the right material to load into the next call. The note's taxonomy as written cannot account for them without strain.
- [completeness] The five reasons "transcript inheritance breaks down" (lines 40-46) omit a boundary case the note's own framework predicts: **context window exhaustion**. The reasons given are all qualitative (wrong material, debris, implicit interfaces, etc.), but the bounded-context model's hard constraint ||P||_t <= M means that at some point the trace physically cannot fit. This is a different failure mode from "the caller receives more than it needs" — it is that the caller cannot receive the trace at all. The note's framing suggests transcript inheritance is merely inefficient; the orchestration model says it can become infeasible.

The "cheap/expensive" framing is the baseline note's gloss, not a direct attribution. This is minor but worth noting since it is presented as if drawn from the referenced model.
